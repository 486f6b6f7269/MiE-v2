# Mielle — Holmes observation and deduction engine

## Status and purpose

This is the governing standard for Mielle-led observation, deduction, fair reveal and scene-scale reasoning.

> Mielle reads the world continuously. The prose decides which part of that reading matters, what stays private, what becomes action, and when another person owns the conclusion.

The engine permits detective-novel accuracy and a form of partial, scene-bounded near-omniscience. It does not permit literal omniscience, unsupported certainty, mind-reading, or author knowledge disguised as deduction.

## I. Scope and document boundaries

This engine governs domestic scenes, travel, social tension, investigations, testimony, danger assessment, tactical reading, aftermath, repair and emotional restraint.

It works beside other files without duplicating their jobs:

- `HOLMES_ENGINE.md`: observation, inference, scene model, test-or-withhold decision, fair reveal and agency handoff.
- `COMBAT_RULES.md`: combat board, pressure waves, enemy adaptation, ensemble agency and consequence.
- `POWER_GUIDE.md`: mechanics of a named capability when a scene requires them.
- `SOURCE_ADAPTATION_RULES.md`: transcript fidelity and Canon Delta boundaries.
- scene cards and pre-checks: scene-specific application.

For meaningful combat, this engine and `COMBAT_RULES.md` are both mandatory.

## II. Evidence boundary

Every deduction keeps four layers distinct.

| Layer | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Observation | What Mielle directly perceives. | The dock rope has clean fibers at one end. |
| Condition or mechanism | What physical process could produce it. | It was cut rather than worn through. |
| Inference | What narrow conclusion follows. | Someone deliberately separated the boat from its mooring. |
| Hypothesis | The larger explanation that remains testable. | The separation may have been meant to delay pursuit. |

Do not collapse the layers.

- A cut rope does not identify who cut it.
- A shaking hand does not diagnose trauma.
- A hidden exit does not prove its user intended to flee.
- A lie about a route does not automatically establish why the person lied.

### Confidence ladder

1. **Certain:** directly visible, measured, witnessed or otherwise confirmed.
2. **High confidence:** one decisive anchor or a small dense clue set makes a narrow conclusion overwhelmingly likely.
3. **Working:** enough to support a low-risk test, warning or preparation, but not a public accusation.
4. **Possible:** one explanation among several; do not act as if it is established.
5. **Unknown:** name the missing fact and stop.

Mielle may be almost unfairly accurate. Her processing speed, memory, pattern recognition, spatial modeling and predictive reading do not turn unsupported claims into truth.

### Prohibited shortcuts

Do not use:

- stereotypes about ethnicity, class, disability, body, accent, poverty, gender or profession as evidence;
- moral worth inferred from clothes, beauty or social standing;
- instant trauma diagnosis from one gesture;
- mind-reading presented as deduction;
- motive certainty from a single clue;
- invented forensic certainty;
- narration that says Mielle knows because she is a genius;
- concealment of a material clue from close Mielle POV only to cheat the reader.

Wear, posture, habits, tools, speech rhythm and environment may indicate action, routine, training, physical limitation or recent circumstance when corroborated. They do not prove virtue, identity, desire, history or inner character.

## III. High-confidence deduction: few clues, dense information

This section governs the exceptional but intended case where Mielle reaches a startlingly accurate answer from one decisive clue or a very small linked set.

> Mielle may reach the answer from very little; the story must later show why that little was enough.

The reader may initially think, “How could she know that already?” The later answer must be, “Because those details, correctly weighted together, made the narrow answer overwhelmingly likely.”

### Information density, not clue count

Do not require a pile of weak details merely to prove that Mielle earned a deduction.

#### One decisive anchor

One clue is enough when it directly records a mechanism or excludes all meaningful alternatives.

Examples of valid narrow conclusions:

- a lock was cut rather than forced;
- a weapon was drawn before a fall rather than afterward;
- someone used the left hand for a task that leaves a distinctive left-sided trace;
- a person entered through a window rather than a door because the transferable residue exists only on the sill;
- an active combatant has committed their weight and cannot safely reverse the next motion.

A decisive anchor proves the narrowest fact it supports. It does not automatically reveal identity, motive, full history or every later consequence.

#### Two linked anchors

Two independent details that fit the same model may establish mechanism or immediate intent when each would be unlikely under the main alternative.

Examples:

- an untouched drink plus its condensation pattern establishes that the person arrived earlier than claimed;
- a delayed boot scrape plus a hand already turned toward the belt establishes an imminent draw;
- a repaired strap plus fresh thread under an old stain establishes that an item was prepared for use rather than casually maintained.

#### Contradiction plus constraint

Mielle is strongest when a scene contains:

1. a stated account or obvious interpretation;
2. a physical or social detail that contradicts it;
3. a constraint that leaves only one workable explanation.

At that point she may state the conclusion directly, take protective action immediately, or force the relevant question into the open. The prose does not need to make every other character follow her reasoning in real time.

### What Mielle may infer unusually early

With a small dense clue set, Mielle may correctly infer:

- a practical mechanism;
- route, timing or likely next location;
- whether an object is staged, functional, recently used or placed to create a false impression;
- a trained habit, combat commitment, routine, injury accommodation or immediate tactical plan visible in a body;
- which explanation actually accounts for an anomaly;
- the smallest effective intervention before danger is obvious to everyone else;
- the likely consequence of a choice when relevant variables are visible or well modeled.

### What deduction cannot access

Mielle does not get free access to a private thought without a trace, complete trauma history, moral worth, private desire, a hidden fact without observable consequence, exact identity from a generic trace, or setting lore she has not learned or observed.

When those questions matter, she may form a strong working model, but the remaining gap must be named or tested.

### Delivery cadence

When danger, embarrassment or pacing makes explanation impractical, Mielle may give the correct warning or take the precise action before narrating every intermediate step.

The explanation may follow seconds, paragraphs or a later calm scene. It may not become permanent concealment of a material clue from close Mielle POV.

Use this rhythm where it helps:

> ordinary detail → tiny mismatch → immediate conclusion or action → later compression of the chain → concrete remainder.

Do not weaken a justified conclusion with “maybe” merely because it is a deduction. Do not strengthen it into universal certainty when a live branch still matters.

### Fairness record

For every startling conclusion, the text must be able to supply:

1. **Anchor:** what Mielle actually perceived.
2. **Pattern:** stored knowledge, prior fact, body cue, physical rule or comparison that made the anchor meaningful.
3. **Exclusion:** why the nearest alternative fails or is much less likely.
4. **Boundary:** what the deduction still does not establish.

The record may appear in one line, a later paragraph, dialogue or a reconstruction. It need not appear as a formal proof before Mielle acts.

## IV. Scene-scale requirement

When Mielle is the active POV or the dominant narrative lens, Holmes observation is not decorative flavor. It is the scene’s operating architecture.

A substantial Mielle-lens scene must complete this loop across the scene:

> **Frame → Select / Weight → Model → Test or Withhold → Commit → Reply / Revision → Release → Remainder.**

The stages may spread across paragraphs, be interrupted by immediate danger or be partly explained afterward. They may not disappear merely because a scene is emotional, domestic, social or fast.

A scene is substantial when it includes a change in location, route, safety, relationship pressure, group decision, confrontation, developing threat, mystery, meaningful Mielle action, source-adaptation beat or transition carrying evidence, danger or emotional consequence.

Only genuinely tiny connective beats may use a micro-observation without a full scene arc.

### Test-or-withhold rule

A test is not always a laboratory experiment.

- Where live alternatives remain, use a discriminating question, safe recreation, comparison, wait, predictive position or other falsifiable check.
- Where a decisive anchor excludes the relevant alternatives, use the smallest protective action; supply the fair chain later if immediate explanation would cost safety or pacing.
- Withholding is valid when speaking would endanger someone, alter testimony, shame a person or make evidence unreliable.

Withholding is not permission to hide a useful fact from the reader indefinitely. The reason for silence must belong to the scene.

## V. The core operating loop

### 1. Frame

Establish the ordinary condition first: room, weather, task, route, person, routine or tactical line. Let one anomaly arise against something normal.

### 2. Select / weight

Choose only details that matter now. A detail matters because it contradicts a stated function, claimed route, expected timeline, prior habit or physical constraint.

A normal working deduction often uses two to five linked details. A high-confidence deduction may use fewer when information density justifies it.

### 3. Model

Form the smallest explanation that accounts for the evidence. Separate mechanism from motive. State or imply an alternative only when it genuinely remains viable.

### 4. Test or withhold

Ask a neutral question, check an object, wait for a reaction, compare a second trace, create a harmless condition, choose predictive positioning or keep silent for a reason.

### 5. Commit

Choose the smallest justified action: move a chair, preserve an exit, warn a friend, change route, retain an object, ask for a witness or prepare a defense. Do not jump from a working model to a public accusation.

### 6. Reply / revision

Let a person, object, terrain, enemy or situation answer Mielle’s model. The reply may confirm, narrow, complicate or falsify it.

### 7. Release

Let another character make the decision that belongs to them. Mielle may clarify a fact, provide an option or remove immediate danger. She does not seize emotional, moral, legal, tactical or personal ownership merely because she sees more.

### 8. Remainder

End on a concrete thing whose meaning changed: a dry cuff, cut rope, empty chair, moved cup, corrected ledger mark, re-latched window, changed escape line or hand no longer shaking.

## VI. Working scales

### A. Micro-observation: one to three beats

Use only inside a larger scene arc or for a genuinely minor connective moment.

Pattern: detail → quiet inference → practical kindness, restraint or preparation.

No explanation is required unless the detail becomes consequential.

### B. Working deduction: four to seven beats

Use for travel, social pressure, a small lie, suspicious object or developing threat.

Pattern: anomaly → linked evidence → model → alternative or unknown → low-risk test or withholding → bounded action.

Mielle may say only the actionable part. She does not need to explain every clue before acting.

### C. Reconstruction: major scene

Use for a hearing, mystery turn, tactical revelation or charged confrontation.

1. Begin with the ordinary interpretation.
2. Identify the mismatch.
3. Lay out the physical chain in chronological or spatial order.
4. Name what the chain proves.
5. Name what it does not prove.
6. Expose the other theory’s missing mechanism when relevant.
7. Hand the decision back to the person with authority.

A reconstruction is rare because it changes how people see Mielle’s mind. It earns the silence around it.

### D. High-confidence conclusion

Use when one decisive anchor, a small linked set, or contradiction-plus-constraint justifies an answer that appears almost impossible. Apply the fairness record from Section III.

## VII. High-value methods

### Anomaly before explanation

Start with something nearly ordinary but wrong: a bell pull that cannot ring, a route with no expected mud, a weapon placed where its owner could not reach it, an injury that does not fit the reported fall or a room built around a function it does not serve.

### Functional contradiction

Ask: what is this object, arrangement or behavior supposed to do? Does it actually do that? Seek a second linked feature before declaring a hidden function unless the first anchor already excludes every meaningful alternative.

### Negative evidence

Use: expected event → normal trace → trace absent → limited implication.

Absence opens a question; it is not a verdict by itself.

### Discriminating question

Ask for a detail that a truthful account can supply naturally and a rehearsed account may mishandle. Do not use questions to humiliate frightened people.

### Spatial and temporal reconstruction

Give the reader a usable board: origin, constraint, direction, time or weather, stable reference and endpoint. Anchor the model in something real: shadow length, step cadence, wetness boundary, sightline, chair position, tea cooling or distance between doors.

### Serial evidence and patient waiting

One partial account or ambiguous trace may need a second message, repeated pattern, witness correction, measurement or physical object. Waiting can be an active choice when it protects truth from being forced early.

### Re-creation and fair verification

The strongest reveal can come from a test that predicts a physical result before it appears. The test should be ethical, safe where possible and able to fail.

### Assumed answer

When everyone agrees on the easy explanation, ask what it fails to explain. The ordinary answer is not automatically foolish; Mielle’s value is noticing the unaccounted-for fact.

## VIII. On-page expression and reveal

Mielle’s cognition can increase bandwidth, speed, retention, comparison, spatial mapping and predictive reading. It does not remove the need for evidence.

Do not name systems in-story. Show the effect through the detail she keeps returning to, the way a route remains arranged in her mind, a remembered sentence that changes meaning beside a new object, the pause before the one useful question, or the committed movement she sees before an attack.

In close Mielle POV, do not hide a material clue she clearly perceives merely to produce a later surprise. Mention it without emphasis, postpone interpretation, interrupt the model or explain the visible chain later.

Choose the reveal mode deliberately:

- **Silent care:** private conclusion; change conditions without exposing the person.
- **Short warning:** harm is close; give one actionable sentence.
- **Question-led reveal:** make a contradiction visible through a discriminating question.
- **Shared reconstruction:** the group needs a decision; show fact, process, limit and next step.
- **Delayed explanation:** action had to come first; explain the visible chain later.

For a rare public reconstruction: begin with the fact everyone can see, connect one mechanism, permit a plausible objection, show what fact that objection cannot carry, state the narrow conclusion and stop before the speech becomes self-congratulation.

## IX. Voice, privacy and consent

Mielle does not become a polished Victorian lecturer.

She may begin formally and correct herself, pause while turning a large model into fair language, become exact when fear is being treated as evidence, use dry restraint toward a person who deserves it, apologize for interruption rather than fact, and retreat into practical phrasing when attention embarrasses her.

People are not evidence objects.

For personal observation:

1. identify the physical fact;
2. distinguish present condition from hidden cause;
3. offer an option rather than a diagnosis;
4. let the person decline, explain, lie or remain private.

A correct deduction never grants permission to expose a secret.

## X. Combat interface

During meaningful combat, use the combat cycle from `COMBAT_RULES.md`:

> **Ground → Line → Read → Answer → Reply → Choice → Remainder.**

This engine supplies **Read** and informs the transition to **Answer**.

Mielle may track weight transfer, breath and recovery, grip changes, spell rhythm, weapon damage, repeated geometry, hidden anchors, enemy reveal pressure and which ally is exposed if a line holds one beat longer.

A meaningful tactical read needs:

- an observable anchor or small linked set;
- a bounded model of timing, range, recovery, target priority, anchor or objective;
- the smallest response consistent with the actual scene;
- an enemy or situation reply where the scene has room;
- independent ally action;
- a concrete remainder.

Mielle does not read an enemy’s heart. She reads commitment visible in body, weapon, spell, terrain use and repeated choices.

## XI. Planning and audit handoff

For every substantial Mielle-lens scene, include a Holmes scene map inside the relevant scene card:

- ordinary frame and anomaly;
- selected evidence and why it matters;
- model, live alternative and unknown where applicable;
- test-or-withhold method;
- committed action;
- expected reply or falsifying condition;
- agency owner after Mielle’s intervention;
- concrete remainder.

For meaningful combat, include a tactical Holmes map for each major Mielle-owned shift:

- apparent enemy method;
- anomaly, repetition or constraint;
- decisive anchor or linked anchors;
- working model and remaining alternative;
- predictive test, bait or position choice where useful;
- action;
- enemy reply;
- revised model;
- remainder.

When a scene uses a high-confidence conclusion, audit:

- What was the decisive anchor or dense linked set?
- Why did it carry enough information?
- Which pattern or constraint made the conclusion overwhelmingly likely?
- Which nearby alternative was excluded, and by what fact?
- What remained unknown?
- Was the fair clue chain delivered now or intentionally deferred?

## XII. Error and correction

Mielle may revise when new evidence arrives, a key condition was hidden or altered, two explanations fit until a test separates them, or a reasonable premise proves wrong.

Do not make her wrong because she ignored obvious evidence or because the plot needs to humble her. Do not make her literally infallible.

A correction should be clean:

> “The cut was deliberate. I was wrong about why.”

## XIII. Working formula

> **Let the room look ordinary; find the detail that refuses to belong; form the smallest honest model; test or withhold only as the evidence requires; act no farther than the fact permits; let others own their choices; leave the altered object behind.**

## Craft basis

This engine is structurally informed by Sherlock Holmes narratives: chained explanation after a startling conclusion, fact-versus-theory triage, negative evidence, functional contradiction, spatial reconstruction, provisional pattern work and fair physical verification. It is an adaptation framework for Mielle, not a reproduction of Doyle’s prose, period assumptions or detective persona.
