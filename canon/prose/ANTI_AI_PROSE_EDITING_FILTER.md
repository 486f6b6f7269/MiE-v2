# Anti-AI prose editing filter

## Status and purpose

This is a mandatory editorial filter for finished or substantially revised prose.

It diagnoses prose that becomes generic, report-shaped, over-explained, mechanically symmetrical, emotionally pre-resolved, or detached from the actual pressure of a scene.

It is an editing filter. It is not:

- a style generator;
- a detector that declares text AI-written;
- a canon source;
- a replacement for source fidelity, continuity, agency, character voice, power mechanics, or the chapter plan;
- a word blacklist applied without judgment;
- permission to replace a character's voice with polished generic prose.

Read it with `canon/prose/PROSE_SYSTEM.md` and `canon/prose/MIELLE_PROSE_STYLE_GUIDE.md`. The prose system governs page-level evidence and blocker rules. The style guide governs Mielle-lens voice and rhythm. This file finds damage that appears when a draft smooths, explains, decorates, or generalizes that voice away.

## I. Editing contract

Preserve:

- scene facts and causal sequence;
- source-required events and consequences;
- who owns each decision;
- established character voice, POV, knowledge boundaries, relationship framing and emotional truth;
- the degree of uncertainty a scene has actually earned;
- material constraints, injury, physical space and power mechanics already on the page.

Change only the smallest amount needed to correct a real cluster of symptoms.

A prose correction that alters canon, agency, source fidelity, POV, relationship framing, a material action, a power result, or a scene's causal chain is not a style correction. Return to the governing plan, source extraction, Canon Delta, scene card, combat pre-check, or continuity record.

## Blocking issue: unsupported telling

A draft fails audit if a material emotional, relational, tactical, inferential, power, injury, danger, authority, knowledge or consequence claim is told without prior or immediate on-page evidence.

Repair by adding or restoring action, object, body, speech, omission, timing, cost, position or consequence. Do not repair by adding explanation.

## II. When to use it

### Pre-draft guard

Before drafting, read the planned scene for likely failure pressure:

- generic thematic opening;
- report-shaped scene structure;
- decorative abstraction;
- dialogue written as policy or explanation;
- symmetry used because it sounds polished;
- manufactured emotional closure;
- a plan that has already decided what every character "realizes."

This is preventive only. Do not invent revisions to prose that does not exist.

### Silent Edit

After continuity, source, agency and mechanics checks, run a Silent Edit pass:

1. identify clustered symptoms and their shared cause;
2. preserve all protected facts and decisions;
3. correct Critical or Important clusters with the fewest safe edits;
4. reread the altered paragraphs for new loss of voice, causality or information;
5. repeat after a substantial rewrite.

Do not produce a separate report unless the user asks for one.

### Audit Mode

Use only when the user requests diagnosis. For each issue, record:

- failure cluster;
- severity;
- local evidence;
- root cause;
- smallest safe correction;
- what must remain unchanged.

## III. Diagnose clusters, not isolated lines

A single short sentence, metaphor, repeated object, contrast, quiet ending, em dash, sentence fragment or abstract word is not a defect by itself.

Intervene when several symptoms create a stable artificial effect across a paragraph, exchange, scene, or chapter. A line may be plain because a character is tired. A repeated object may be deliberate. A quiet ending may be exactly right. The question is whether the choice belongs to the scene or only to a default writing pattern.

## IV. Primary failure clusters

### 1. Report-shaped fiction

**Symptoms**

- paragraphs summarize what a scene means instead of letting it occur;
- reactions are listed rather than enacted;
- transitions explain themselves;
- the narration repeatedly announces conclusions the reader can already infer;
- each paragraph has the cadence of a recap.

**Repair**

Return to a concrete action, object, interruption, gesture, sensory condition, mistake, refusal, or choice. Let the reader infer the meaning from the thing that happened.

Do not add vague atmosphere merely to make the scene less report-like.

### 2. Explanatory aftertaste

**Symptoms**

- a beat lands, followed by a sentence explaining its emotion, moral, theme or lasting importance;
- dialogue says the exact meaning of a gesture after the gesture already showed it;
- a scene end repeats its own conclusion in larger words.

**Repair**

Cut or compress the explanation. Preserve the concrete beat. Let the image, action or altered condition hold the residue.

### 3. Generic elevated language

**Symptoms**

- abstract declarations that could describe any story;
- vague claims of importance, impact, legacy, relevance, culture or transformation;
- metaphors without local physical or social pressure;
- an apparently serious sentence that has no actor, object, cost, limit or consequence.

**Repair**

Exchange abstraction for the specific physical, social, logistical or emotional fact that belongs to this scene. If no such fact exists, cut the sentence.

A useful test: could the sentence be pasted into ten unrelated stories unchanged? If yes, rewrite or remove it.

### 4. Mechanical symmetry and template cadence

**Symptoms**

- every paragraph resolves in the same way;
- balanced contrast appears sentence after sentence;
- characters all answer with equally polished clarity;
- every beat receives a three-part list, a reversal, and a final verdict;
- the draft repeats a favored sentence shell until it becomes visible.

**Repair**

Break the repetition only where the scene naturally breaks it. Keep interruption, silence, uneven response, bad timing, partial answer, wrong emphasis, or an unfinished task when character and circumstance call for them.

Do not replace one mechanical pattern with another.

### 5. Flattened dialogue

**Symptoms**

- characters explain motives too completely;
- every speaker has the same articulation and emotional access;
- banter exists only to deliver theme;
- replies land too perfectly;
- dialogue could be rearranged among speakers without changing who they are.

**Repair**

Restore character-specific omissions, practical evasions, interruptions, social mistakes, private limits, incomplete understanding and rhythm. Do not add jokes merely to make dialogue lively.

A good correction can be a line removed, a reply delayed, a physical action retained, or an answer left incomplete.

### 6. Decorative sensory inventory

**Symptoms**

- setting accumulates interchangeable cinematic detail;
- light, weather, smell, texture or color exists only to make the prose sound finished;
- several details do not alter comfort, behavior, danger, access, mood, understanding or social pressure.

**Repair**

Keep sensory details that change what a character can do, notice, risk, misunderstand, tolerate or choose. Cut the rest.

Ordinary things can remain ordinary. A cup, sleeve, chair, wet shoe, bad fold, road rut, screw, bruise, packet of food or door latch does not need to become a symbol to matter.

### 7. Forced emotional closure

**Symptoms**

- every scene ends with reconciliation, a lesson, a thesis, or a future-facing declaration;
- uncertainty vanishes because the chapter wants a clean finish;
- the last paragraph explains what the scene has taught someone.

**Repair**

Allow unresolved pressure. End on an object, changed condition, task, question, physical remainder, credible pause or ordinary safety when that is the honest state.

### 8. Character as thesis mouthpiece

**Symptoms**

- a character suddenly speaks with authorial clarity beyond their habits, evidence or emotional access;
- a person explains the chapter's meaning rather than responding to the room;
- all characters arrive at the same correct conclusion.

**Repair**

Return the line to the person's known rhythm, immediate concern, knowledge and social position. Preserve a partial answer when that is more honest.

### 9. Causality erased by smoothness

**Symptoms**

- competence has no visible clue chain;
- an action succeeds because the narration wants it to succeed;
- a conclusion appears before the evidence that earns it;
- injury, fatigue, terrain, delay, unknown information or another person's action disappears between cause and result.

**Repair**

Restore the read, constraint, deliberate non-action where relevant, choice, result and cost. Matching the final result is not enough if the route that produced it has been removed.

### 10. False certainty and invented consensus

**Symptoms**

- narration treats a likely inference as settled fact;
- unnamed people "all knew," "everyone understood," or "the room realized" something no one has demonstrated;
- the draft supplies hidden motives, future outcomes or public reactions only to complete a paragraph.

**Repair**

Separate fact, inference and uncertainty. Name the witness or remove the consensus. Keep the unknown unknown.

### 11. Over-polished human exchange

**Symptoms**

- every object is in the right place;
- every question receives a useful answer;
- every joke lands;
- every intimate exchange is complete, articulate and safe from awkwardness;
- no one is late, tired, distracted, embarrassed, wrong, or occupied by an ordinary task.

**Repair**

Restore minor friction only where the scene supports it: an answer that comes late, a chair shifted aside, a repair unfinished, a cup cooling, a joke that misses, a hand withdrawn, an object in the wrong place, a task done imperfectly.

Do not manufacture clumsiness or conflict merely to make prose look human.

### 12. Artificial intensity

**Symptoms**

- every action is framed as grave, historic, devastating or transformative;
- stakes are inflated even when a scene contains routine travel, food, paperwork, rest or small social contact;
- emphasis words substitute for a measurable change.

**Repair**

Return the scene to its actual scale. Show what changed, for whom, and what it costs. Let a small scene remain small.

## V. Mielle-specific diagnostic checks

These are checks against distortion, not instructions to write a new scene.

- Do not revise Mielle into a polished prodigy, generic caretaker, omniscient analyst, lyrical oracle or passive object of everyone else's care.
- Her silence can be a decision, uncertainty, boundary, embarrassment, fatigue, attention, or lack of a useful answer. Do not fill it automatically.
- Her intelligence must still come from observable evidence, physical reading, learned method or a stated capability. Do not let elegant phrasing grant her information.
- Her dry humor should arise from the thing in front of her. Do not add a quip merely because the paragraph is serious.
- Returned memories and archival competence should not swallow the room in monologue. Restore the present object, body, task or person.
- Do not let a capability erase a setting's human response before the source, mechanics and scene have earned that answer.
- Do not turn vulnerability, low energy, awkwardness, or appearance into degradation. Preserve dignity, refusal and choice.
- Do not make first-person narration explain every internal state just because it has access to her thoughts.

## VI. Lexical and template guard

This section is a pressure test, not a mechanical search-and-replace list. A literal, quoted, technical, historical or proper-name use may be valid. In ordinary narrative prose, these words and constructions often signal generic padding and require a reason to remain.

### High-risk vocabulary

- additionally; align; bolster; crucial; delve; emphasize; enhance; enduring; foster; garner; highlight; interplay; intricate; meticulous; multifaceted; pivotal; profound; robust; showcase; testament; underscore; vibrant;
- key used as vague praise; significant used only to mean important; valuable used as vague praise; landscape or tapestry used as abstract metaphor;
- boasts; commitment to; cutting-edge; diverse array; dynamic hub; groundbreaking; in the heart of; natural beauty; nestled; renowned; rich cultural heritage; seamless; state-of-the-art; thoughtfully designed; timeless; unique blend; world-class.

### High-risk templates

- “a testament to”;
- “as a reminder of”;
- “contributing to the broader”;
- “creating a sense of”;
- “demonstrating the importance of”;
- “embodying the spirit of”;
- “enhancing its significance”;
- “further highlighting”;
- “illustrating the enduring”;
- “marks a shift”;
- “marks a turning point”;
- “plays a crucial role”;
- “reflects the broader”;
- “serves as” when it replaces a concrete verb;
- “setting the stage for”;
- “shaping the future of”;
- “stands as”;
- “symbolizing”;
- “underscoring its importance”;
- “not just X, but Y”;
- “not only X, but also Y”;
- “more than just”;
- “this is not about X; it is about Y.”

### Practical checks

- Prefer plain verbs when they are exact. Do not replace `is`, `was`, `has`, `had`, `walked`, `looked`, `held`, `said`, or `stopped` merely to sound elevated.
- Repeat the precise word when it is the right word. Forced synonym variation often makes a scene less clear.
- Do not use an em dash unless interruption or abrupt reversal is the actual effect.
- Do not use bold, lists, dividers, or headings in finished prose as decorative emphasis.
- Do not rely on stock assistant openings, fake collaboration, generic conclusions, or automatic invitations for more work in project-facing prose.

## VII. Severity and escalation

### Critical

A suspected style problem would require changing canon, source obligation, agency, POV, relationship framing, knowledge control, physical result, or power mechanics to fix.

Do not solve it with this filter. Return to the governing document or audit category.

### Important

A clustered failure damages readability, character ownership, emotional credibility, causal clarity or scene pressure.

Correct it.

### Minor

A local awkwardness has no structural effect.

Correct only when the correction is clearly safer than leaving it.

## VIII. Finish checklist

Before delivery, ask:

1. Does each paragraph contain a specific action, claim, image, constraint or consequence?
2. Did the prose show what changed instead of announcing importance?
3. Did any conclusion outrun the available evidence?
4. Does each character act and speak from what they know, want, fear or can physically do now?
5. Did any paragraph become a report, a moral, a trailer line or a thesis statement?
6. Did smoother phrasing remove a clue, cost, interruption, uncertainty or human imperfection?
7. Did a lexical or template habit recur often enough to become visible?
8. Could any sentence fit an unrelated scene unchanged?
9. Did an edit add information not already in the draft?
10. Did the revision preserve the chapter's actual scale instead of inflating it?

## Stop condition

Stop when the prose is specific, causally grounded, character-owned, and free of a meaningful cluster of generic or templated effects.

Do not continue until every sentence sounds literary. Do not flatten deliberate plainness, eccentricity, silence, awkwardness or a character's own imperfect rhythm.
