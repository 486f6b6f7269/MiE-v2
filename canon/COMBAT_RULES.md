# Combat rules

## Status and purpose

This is the governing combat-writing standard for *Mielle in Exandria*.

Combat is a conflict of position, attention, pressure, choice and consequence. It is not a transcription of RPG turns.

The desired result is combat that is:

- technically legible;
- precise when Mielle acts;
- grounded in distance, terrain, attention and physical consequence;
- protective when protection is the actual choice;
- faithful to Mielle’s capability without turning every conflict into her answer alone;
- faithful to canon character agency and source-event structure.

Mielle may sometimes feel almost unfair to an opponent because her perception, memory, spatial awareness and predictive combat reading are extraordinary. The prose must still establish why she knows what she knows.

## I. Source boundary

The source transcript establishes outcomes, meaningful decisions, information flow, tactical intent and later consequences unless an approved Canon Delta changes them.

Dice results, initiative order, repeated attack rolls, rules debate and map-square movement are table artifacts unless a later consequence depends on them.

Before adapting a fight, identify:

- the source beat that must survive;
- whose agency must remain intact;
- what the encounter changes after it ends;
- which table artifacts should disappear from prose.

## II. Every fight needs a governing idea

Every meaningful fight must answer one sentence before prose begins:

> **What is the tactical truth that decides this conflict?**

Examples:

- The archer must exhale before correcting for a moving target.
- The creature’s charge works only while both feet remain on one line.
- The illusion repeats the caster’s delayed shoulder movement.
- Four attackers are not four equal threats: two are bait, one carries the object, one is the actual shot.
- The swordsman’s first strike is not the danger; the second hand is.

Do not begin with generic danger. Begin with the rule that makes the danger intelligible.

## III. Speed, distance and timing

Speed becomes useful when it changes distance or timing. It is not a synonym for “Mielle wins.”

Mielle may gain a beat by:

- taking half a step instead of one;
- turning a shoulder instead of retreating;
- moving someone a few inches rather than throwing them across a room;
- redirecting a wrist rather than meeting a weapon head-on;
- allowing an attack to pass where it is harmless and using its committed line;
- entering a range too close for a long weapon and too far for an immediate grapple;
- letting a threat believe it found a gap, then revealing that the gap was measured.

Write physical cause before result.

Weak: “Mielle was too fast for him.”

Useful: “His knife had committed to the space her ribs had occupied. She had turned before his wrist finished following it.”

## IV. The opponent needs a combat language

An enemy is not strong in the abstract. Give them a repeatable operating logic.

Possible combat languages:

- wide destructive commitment;
- cautious probing;
- crowding and clinch pressure;
- distance traps;
- ranged suppression;
- false openings;
- fear or charm pressure;
- body duplication;
- armor that rewards brute contact;
- a predator pursuing isolated prey;
- a duelist trying to force a reveal;
- a caster who needs a rhythm, focus or line of sight.

Mielle reads that logic through physical anchors:

- feet;
- weight distribution;
- breath;
- grip;
- shoulder and hip timing;
- damaged armor;
- weapon wear;
- spell preparation rhythm;
- where someone keeps looking;
- target preference;
- terrain use.

She may reach a correct conclusion quickly, but prose must either show the clue chain immediately or show the result first and explain the clues soon afterward.

## V. Containment is active

Mielle’s restraint must not read as inability.

Containment can mean:

- holding one local sector;
- deflecting rather than breaking;
- disabling a hand, knee, balance, weapon or route;
- removing an ally from a line of fire;
- using bokken precision to interrupt posture;
- moving only enough to make an attack miss;
- preserving civilian safety;
- preventing escalation in front of people who do not need to see everything she can do;
- waiting until an enemy’s actual method is visible.

The scene must communicate that Mielle is choosing the smallest truthful answer, not losing until a late rescue.

A contained Mielle should produce one or more real effects:

- an enemy cannot understand why each line fails;
- allies gain room to act;
- a civilian is not hurt;
- a pattern becomes visible;
- an enemy overcommits because they misread her;
- the group keeps an option that brute force would destroy.

This is a prose and agency rule. It is not a power-access tier or a restriction on what Mielle is allowed to have.

## VI. Escalation requires a changed condition

A fight escalates because a fact changes:

- the enemy learns what Mielle is protecting;
- a second weapon, anchor or method appears;
- a civilian enters the line;
- terrain collapses or narrows;
- a direct supernatural effect changes the conflict;
- the earlier answer no longer protects the people involved;
- another character creates a new opening;
- the enemy changes target, range, objective or method;
- a hidden rule of the encounter becomes visible.

Do not escalate because the paragraph needs to be louder.

## VII. The decisive action is usually simpler than the setup

Careful observation can end in one redirected cut, a small turn of the bokken, a moved elbow, a broken line of sight, a disarm, a short warning or an opening another character uses.

The decisive motion should feel inevitable in retrospect. It does not need to be large.

## VIII. Use other POVs to show the mismatch

Mielle’s own perspective should remain concrete and non-boastful. She often sees mechanics too clearly to call herself impressive.

A brief witness or opponent beat may show what she has done:

- an enemy realizes the counter was timed to the decision before the swing;
- an ally notices Mielle never lost track of the second attacker;
- a caster sees that she left them room to finish a spell;
- a stranger realizes that the apparently small, tired girl had been managing the escape line.

Do not make the group applaud in the middle of a fight. Let the recognition settle later.

## IX. End with consequence, not a pose

Every fight leaves a remainder:

- a broken arrow;
- a strip of cloth tied around a wrist;
- blood on a worn bokken;
- an enemy who chose to flee;
- a damaged cart;
- a split knuckle;
- a spell that will not be cast again;
- a civilian who now asks a different question;
- a question the group postpones until the road is quieter.

The aftermath must preserve agency. Someone else may choose to interrogate, arrest, forgive, flee, kill, apologize, pay, report, or continue.

## X. Mielle-specific non-negotiables

### A. Role and position

Mielle has no permanent fixed combat station in v2. Her lane is determined by the source situation, current relationship state, terrain, immediate exposure and the agency that must remain with other people.

Her first tactical question is not “How do I defeat everyone?” It is:

> **Who is in immediate danger, what line reaches them first, and what is the smallest intervention that keeps their own choice possible?**

She may protect a person nearby, hold an exit, intercept a flank, deny a ranged angle, extract someone, read the enemy or take a direct duel. Do not force her into a fixed backline role when the scene does not support it.

### B. Defensive truthfulness

Before a hostile effect reaches Mielle, check the actual mechanics that apply.

Do not impose surprise, injury, restraint, charm, fear, coercion, possession, draining, illusion, soul interference or “too many attackers” merely because the plot needs a setback.

At the same time, do not invent a defense or an outcome that the established capability does not support. State the mechanism: negated, detected, resisted, mitigated, conditional or effective.

A meaningful threat needs conditions: multi-vector pressure, terrain, a civilian, unfamiliar interference, competing obligations, a concealed mechanism, a moral restraint, an opponent who has changed the board, or a source consequence that must be preserved.

### C. Scale of visible action

Do not use fixed tiers, recovery percentages or legacy visibility locks.

Choose the least intrusive action that honestly preserves the scene’s safety, source obligations and ensemble agency. A small action is not automatically better than a large one; use the action the situation actually requires.

Do not name author-side systems in dialogue or story prose. Show their effect through action and consequence.

### D. Mielle’s combat intellect

Mielle may process physical cues, spatial geometry, tactical history, pattern repetition, memory comparison, likely reactions and fine timing very quickly.

She cannot:

- read unsupported motives as facts;
- identify a hidden controller without evidence;
- know a secret because the plot needs a reveal;
- predict a choice without behavioral foundation;
- erase uncertainty by saying she simply knew.

Use this distinction:

- **Fast certainty:** the knife will come from the left; the attacker has already shifted weight.
- **Bounded uncertainty:** the left hand is hidden; it could hold a second knife, a focus or nothing. She must not assume which.

## XI. Combat cycle

Use this seven-part cycle for meaningful action scenes.

### 1. Ground — establish the board

Begin with a material anchor: mud pulling at boots, wet cobbles, a torch throwing moving shadows, a wagon wheel, rain on a bowstring, a tavern table between people, ozone before a spell, a too-quiet tree line or a ceiling too low for a long weapon.

State positions cleanly:

- Mielle;
- active allies;
- immediate threats;
- civilians or vulnerable people;
- exits and terrain limits;
- lines of sight and hostile vectors.

### 2. Line — identify the immediate danger

Show the hostile line in a concrete form: arrow path, sword angle, spell source, charging body, social or mental interference, collapsing object, line of sight or escape route.

Mielle does not need to name every threat. She needs to identify the one that matters first.

### 3. Read — build the combat hypothesis

Use three or fewer concrete cues where possible.

Example:

- The archer’s left heel is not planted.
- The bowstring is wet.
- His eyes keep checking the cart wheel.

Inference: he needs the cart to keep a target still long enough for a second shot.

Alternative: he may only be choosing cover.

Action: move the threatened person before the shot, but do not rush the tree line until the model is tested.

### 4. Answer — make the smallest effective correction

Possible answers:

- half step;
- hand on shoulder;
- bokken turn;
- deflection;
- foot sweep;
- broken line of sight;
- short pull;
- barrier;
- disarm;
- redirecting a threat into an ally’s chosen line;
- one warning.

Do not stack five abilities where one truthful action works.

### 5. Reply — let the enemy or scene change

The opponent must not repeat a failed plan without consequence. They may change range, reveal a concealed weapon, sacrifice safety for speed, choose a hostage, call reinforcements, exploit terrain, retreat intelligently, force a moral choice or identify what Mielle is doing.

This creates pressure without pretending Mielle forgot her capabilities.

### 6. Choice — decide escalation and cost

The decisive question is ethical and tactical:

> What does Mielle refuse to let happen, and what is she willing to risk or reveal to stop it?

A larger response requires a real line crossing: imminent lethal harm, an effect the current answer cannot safely contain, a person with no credible exit, a threat that changes the local battlefield, or a source consequence that cannot be preserved otherwise.

### 7. Remainder — show what changed

After victory, escape or interruption:

- return to a physical object or altered space;
- assign the next choice to another character;
- let Mielle’s fatigue, awkwardness, silence or concern reappear;
- do not end on a power pose.

## XII. Fight architectures

### 1. Short interception

Use for arrows, flanks, lone chargers, simple attackers or a narrow tactical moment.

Pattern:

1. Ground: someone is occupied or exposed.
2. Line: a rear or side threat commits.
3. Read: one visible cue gives Mielle early warning.
4. Answer: minimal deflection, disarm, extraction or redirection.
5. Party beat: another character acts on the room created.
6. Remainder: the threat is gone, disabled or forced away; the other person continues their own role.

Mielle does not need a monologue. The scene should continue moving.

### 2. Technical duel

Use for a competent enemy whose method is worth reading.

Pattern:

1. The opponent’s combat language appears.
2. Mielle gathers data rather than ending the exchange immediately.
3. The opponent mistakes this for pressure or weakness.
4. Each exchange removes one unnecessary movement from Mielle’s defense.
5. The opponent adapts.
6. Mielle identifies the bottleneck.
7. A clean restrained action ends the duel or creates an ally’s opening.

The duel is not Mielle lecturing someone weaker. Dialogue remains sparse, practical and character-specific.

### 3. Multi-vector protection fight

Use when the difficulty is choosing safe routes and preserving several people’s agency.

Requirement: establish at least four or five meaningful pressures or an equivalent structure. “Several attackers” is not enough.

Possible pressure map:

- one threat occupies the front;
- one threatens a specific person;
- one threatens a civilian or another ally;
- one has range or line of sight;
- one changes terrain or restricts exit.

The problem is not merely whether Mielle can win. It is which person gets which safe route, who must own their lane, and which pressure cannot be addressed without changing the scene.

### 4. Rule-breaking supernatural fight

Use for curses, possession, forced inevitability, strange magic, identity pressure or world-rule imposition.

Pattern:

1. Show the hostile mechanism.
2. Classify its effect on Mielle honestly: negated, detected, resisted, mitigated, conditional or effective.
3. Let the failed effect become information.
4. Identify the actual anchor, cost or limitation.
5. Use the smallest response that breaks the imposed rule.
6. Preserve witness reaction, political consequence, evidence and changed knowledge.

Resistance is not a free skip. It changes the question.

### 5. Emergency display

Use when a direct, local danger makes lower-impact action dishonest.

Pattern:

1. Earlier options have visibly failed or are demonstrably inadequate.
2. A real condition makes further restraint unsafe.
3. Mielle acts with a protective purpose.
4. The visible action is scoped to the actual danger.
5. The scene spends time on reaction and consequence rather than a victory lap.

## XIII. POV and prose rules

### Mielle POV

Mielle’s fight prose is concrete, calm under pressure, attentive to small mechanics, emotionally honest without melodrama and economical at the point of impact.

She does not internally announce superiority. She notices things like:

- “His shoulder rose before the blade did.”
- “The second arrow was for the place she would run.”
- “The spell had a rhythm. Its third pulse lagged.”
- “The left foot slipped. The line was open for one breath.”

Use short decisive sentences at impact.

### Enemy POV

Use only when it reveals a mismatch.

- They thought Mielle was reacting to the swing; she was reacting to the decision before it.
- They thought they forced her back; she moved them away from the person they wanted.
- They thought she avoided lethal force because she was weak; she was sorting the pattern.
- They thought she saw one attack; she had been watching the hidden hand.

Keep it brief. Do not repeatedly make enemies announce that Mielle is a monster.

### Ally POV

Use it to preserve ensemble agency.

- An ally sees an opening and takes it.
- A caster uses a space Mielle cleared and owns the spell.
- A rogue chooses the killing angle.
- A practical fighter reads the result and redirects the group.
- A support character remains active, frightened, funny, caring, angry or brave in their own way.

## XIV. What must not be imported

Do not import:

- self-mythologizing protagonist narration;
- theatrical catchphrases;
- combat as a performance for spectators;
- casual slaughter as a joke;
- prolonged invulnerability displays;
- sexualized combat framing;
- the idea that every opponent exists to certify Mielle’s greatness;
- deliberately accepting a wound because it looks impressive;
- arbitrary final explosions;
- a party that becomes decorative around an overpowered lead.

Mielle’s restraint is care, ethics, fatigue, privacy and tactical judgment. She may be dry or cutting toward someone who deserves it. She is not cruel for applause.

## XV. Mandatory combat pre-check

Before drafting a fight, answer every question concretely:

1. **Scene ownership:** What source beat and whose agency must remain intact?
2. **Board:** Where are Mielle, allies, enemies, civilians, exits and terrain limits?
3. **Immediate protection:** Who is exposed first, and why?
4. **Hostile-effect review:** What happens if each meaningful effect reaches Mielle, and why?
5. **Tactical truth:** What single proposition decides the conflict?
6. **Enemy language:** What does the opponent repeatedly try to do?
7. **Mielle’s clue chain:** What does she perceive, infer, doubt and verify?
8. **Smallest honest response:** What action preserves the scene without unnecessary excess?
9. **Agency:** What meaningful action does each active ally retain?
10. **Escalation trigger:** What exact fact changes the answer?
11. **Consequence:** What physical, emotional, legal, social or strategic remainder survives?
12. **Witness knowledge:** Who sees enough to change how they understand Mielle, and what remains unknown to them?

Do not draft until each answer is concrete.

## XVI. Draft audit

After the scene, verify:

- [ ] The opening establishes terrain and positions.
- [ ] Every major action has a physical cause and result.
- [ ] Mielle’s knowledge comes from evidence, exceptional processing or a later-explained clue chain.
- [ ] Mielle does not become omniscient about motives or secrets.
- [ ] The hostile-effect review was honored.
- [ ] Mielle’s position follows the actual board rather than a fixed role.
- [ ] Present allies retain their own choices.
- [ ] The enemy adapts after a failed tactic.
- [ ] Escalation follows a changed condition.
- [ ] Visible power or unusual action is justified, scoped and consequential.
- [ ] No author-side system name appears in story prose or dialogue.
- [ ] The fight does not treat ability as a joke, vanity exercise or excuse to erase tension.
- [ ] The scene ends with a concrete remainder and a human consequence.

## Working formula

> **Ground the board; identify the line; let Mielle read the mechanism; answer with the smallest honest movement; let the enemy adapt; make escalation a choice; let the group finish what belongs to them; leave a remainder.**
