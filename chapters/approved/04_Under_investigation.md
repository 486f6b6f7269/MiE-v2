# Chapter 04 — Under investigation

The wooden sword stayed where I had put it.

A boot passed close enough to push loose dirt over the wooden edge, stopped, and moved on when the guard found a bloodier thing to worry about. No one picked the sword up. No one handed it back. It lay flat in the lantern light with the other lowered weapons and looked, for once, exactly like what it was: a piece of wood somebody had decided not to trust.

That was safer than the truth, so I let it stay there.

The Crownsguard made the tent smaller by entering it. Not physically. The canvas walls did not move, the center pole still held, the performers' flap still breathed a little cold night air whenever somebody passed behind it. But the paths through the room closed one by one. A shield at the main flap. A crossbow near the aisle. A guard with a ledger standing where people had been trying to run. The crowd that remained stopped being a crowd and became rows of witnesses, workers, bodies, weapons, questions.

Jester was still on my left.

A broad man in Crownsguard colors came through the entrance after the first line of shields, and the other guards made room for him before he asked for it. Vermilion cloth, bronze catching lantern light, helmet under one arm, face set in the way of a person who had already decided that surprise was a luxury for later. The guard who had first ordered weapons down turned toward him and spoke low. I caught only pieces: two beasts, carnival, killed, girl, swords, not leaving.

The broad man looked at the old man on the ground. Then at the young woman. Then at Yasha's greatsword in the dirt.

After that his eyes went to the people still standing.

"Who runs this show?"

Gustav came forward because no one else did. He had the look of a man who had spent years turning disasters into inconveniences and had just found the first one that would not change shape for him. His coat was torn at one cuff. He kept that hand folded inside the other as if the cuff were the problem.

"I do," he said.

Bo stood behind him, pale around the mouth. Molly was a pace off to the side, bright coat marked with dirt, both hands visible, no swords in them. He looked less like a performer than he had an hour ago and more like a man making a calculation over a table with no good cards left. When a guard pointed him toward Gustav and Bo, he went. With one tilt of his chin that said he understood which side of the line he had been placed on and hated it with style to spare.

"Carnival folk there," the Watchmaster said. "Everyone else stays where I can see hands. Flynn. You first."

The frightened guard from before swallowed hard enough that I saw it from across the dirt. Flynn, then. He kept one hand on his sword hilt and one finger out toward the bodies, as if pointing at them again could keep them from becoming anything else.

"I saw one of them," he said. "The old man. It just... it changed. It killed her, and then she changed too. The carnival did something. It had to."

"You saw the carnival do it?"

Flynn's mouth opened.

Nothing came out for half a second.

"I saw it happen here."

The Watchmaster wrote something in the ledger without looking forgiving about the difference.

That was the first useful thing he did. He did not know the cause. He also did not pretend that panic had supplied it.

The next part came in fragments because the room was still being divided. Guards took names from people who wanted to be anywhere else. A woman with blood on her sleeve said she had only come to watch the show. A man kept repeating that he had not touched a weapon until a guard told him that no one had asked that yet. Someone near the back started crying again and stopped when another guard asked whether she needed to sit or whether she could stand and answer.

I kept my hands empty and visible. My wooden sword stayed on the ground.

Jester gave her name when asked, then added the Traveler's with enough brightness that Fjord's head turned a fraction. The guard writing names did not look up at the symbol on her belt. He was busy deciding how to spell things in a room full of people talking too fast.

"Fjord," Fjord said when his turn came. He stood like someone who had made himself part of the answer before the question had finished. Calm enough to be useful. Not calm enough to be careless.

"Beauregard," Beau said. "Beau. Either works if it gets us out of the murder tent faster."

The guard's quill paused.

"It is not getting you out of anything yet."

"Didn't think so," she said, and looked away before the argument could become a second one.

Caleb gave his name quietly. Nott stood close enough to him that the edge of his coat hid part of her mask from the nearest guard.

"Nott," she said. "The Brave. That is part of it. You should write all of it, probably. For accuracy."

The guard looked at her, then at Caleb, then wrote less than all of it.

Yasha had said almost nothing. She stood with her arms loose at her sides, too large for the space the guards wanted her to occupy and too still for anyone to forget. When the Watchmaster asked if anyone knew where the girl from the performance had gone, her eyes lifted.

"Toya," she said.

The name landed harder than the amount of sound should have allowed.

"You know where she is?"

"Maybe."

"Flynn," the Watchmaster said. "Take her. Bring the girl here. No hero work. No detours."

Flynn did not like that order. Yasha liked it less. Neither of them argued. Yasha's eyes passed once over the line of lowered weapons. They stopped on the wooden sword. Then they moved to me.

She had seen it leave her custody. I had known that already. Seeing her remember it while a guard stood between us was worse than knowing.

She did not ask. She followed Flynn out through the press of guards and canvas shadow, and the tent lost one quiet thing that had been holding part of it steady.

The Watchmaster turned back to the rest of us.

"You lot." His quill pointed, not quite at Fjord, not quite at Jester, but at the shape the guards had made of us. "You helped bring the fiends down?"

"Yes," Fjord said.

"We helped," Jester added, and then, because she was Jester, "They were very bad."

The Watchmaster's face did not change.

"You know what caused it?"

No one answered quickly.

That was better than any answer we had.

"No," Caleb said at last. One word, careful enough not to grow branches.

"No," Fjord said. "We saw the transformation when everyone else did."

Beau made a short sound in her throat, almost a laugh and not a happy one. "If we knew what caused it, we'd be having a different conversation."

The Watchmaster looked at her long enough to mark the edge in it, then let it pass.

I said nothing. My answer would have been the same way as theirs and less useful because I had no local words to put around it. Curse. Disease. Spell. Trick. Divine punishment. Bad carnival machine. In a room that still smelled of burned hide and dirt and metal, each word felt like a door I did not have the right key for.

The Watchmaster wrote again.

"On behalf of the Starosta, your help is noted," he said. "It does not clear you. You are all part of this investigation until told otherwise. You will not leave Trostenwald. If you try, the Crownsguard will come after you, and I will be less grateful when we find you."

Nott made a small noise behind Caleb's coat.

"Noted," Fjord said, before anyone else could make that noise larger.

"Where are you staying?"

There it was. A simple question, finally. The sort of question a person could ask over breakfast or at an inn counter, with a hand already reaching for a ledger because every answer in a town was supposed to fit into a line.

"Nestled Nook," Fjord said.

The quill moved.

"All of you?"

Fjord's eyes moved once, not to me. To the people who had actually arrived with him, or close enough: Jester, Beau, Caleb, Nott. Molly had already been put on the other side of the room. Yasha was gone with Flynn. I was not in his answer. I should not have been.

"That's where we're staying," he said.

It was a good answer. Short. True enough for the people it belonged to. It left nothing extra hanging from it.

Then the Watchmaster looked at me.

Not all the way at first. His eyes went to the red hood, the closed lens, the empty hands, the place on the dirt where the wooden sword had been noted and not explained. I was too near Jester to be outside the way of the answer and too far from everyone else to be safely inside it.

"And her?"

My throat tried to close before my mouth had chosen a word.

There were honest answers. That was the problem. I had used a place no one had given me. I had slept where I had not been invited, in a building whose name I did not know, in a town whose law I did not understand. I could say abandoned and learn whether abandoned meant available or trespass. I could say alone and watch the guard decide what kind of problem that made me. I could say I have somewhere and leave him to ask where. Every truthful fragment opened another place for the quill to go.

My hand moved toward my skirt as if a pocket could contain a better law.

It did not. I stopped it halfway.

"I was..." The words came out too neat and too small. "I had a temporary arrangement."

Beau's head turned.

Caleb's did not, but his fingers closed once on the edge of his coat near Nott's shoulder.

The Watchmaster's quill stopped above the page.

"Where?"

Jester stepped on the question before I could bleed on it.

"She's with us, she just came from a really really distant place and now she is staying with us" she said.

Too quick. Too warm. Too certain for a thing we had not agreed to be.

The Watchmaster looked at her.

Jester looked back as if she had just remembered a normal fact and was helping everyone catch up. "At the Nestled Nook. She is with us at the Nestled Nook."

Fjord's jaw moved once. Not a word. A measurement.

Nott whispered, "The breakfast place," which was not helpful and somehow was.

Beau looked at me then, and the look was sharp enough to cut thread. Not proof. Not kindness. A person finding the false board in a floor and choosing, for the moment, not to stomp on it.

Caleb watched the Watchmaster instead.

That was probably the smartest thing anyone did.

The Watchmaster looked from Jester to me. "Is that so?"

The answer should have been easy. It was not. Easy answers belonged to people who had decided what they were before the room asked them to write it down.

Jester's shoulder was a few inches from mine. She did not lean into me. She did not make the answer for me again. The space stayed open, and that made the next breath worse.

I nodded once.

The quill did not move.

"Girl," the Watchmaster said. "Words."

"Yes," I said.

It was not where I had slept. It was where the law could find me now. Those were different facts, and the room wanted only one of them.

The quill scratched. Mielle, if he had caught the name from someone. Nestled Nook, certainly. A place I had eaten because Jester paid. A place I had left before dark because it had not belonged to me. A place that now had enough weight to pull me toward it through the guard's ink.

No one contradicted her.

That was not the same as agreement. Fjord's silence was a practical thing, held steady. Beau's was a knife left sheathed. Caleb's was a door kept closed because opening it would let in more soldiers. Nott's silence was less complete; she breathed something into Caleb's coat that might have been, "One more," or might have been a prayer against paperwork. Jester's silence with a smile on her face, after the cover, was the strangest. She had put the roof over me and then stopped talking before she turned it into a house.

The Watchmaster shut the ledger halfway, one finger keeping his place.

"Nestled Nook, then. You stay there. If you make trouble, if you leave town, if any of you decide this was a night best forgotten, you will make my work easier by making yourselves look guilty."

"We understand," Fjord said.

"Do you?"

No one answered that. Understanding was too large a claim for the amount of night we had survived.

A guard behind the Watchmaster called from the carnival side. Gustav stood with Bo and Molly under a second line of shields. Molly had one hand lifted, palm open, not surrendering so much as reminding everyone that he knew what surrender was supposed to look like. His eyes found the group for half a second. They found Jester. Then, because he was Molly, he made a small bow with only his fingers.

It did not free him. It did make one of the guards frown, which I suspected was the best he could afford.

"They stay," the Watchmaster said without looking back. "The rest of you, out. We know where to find you."

The guard line opened toward the main flap. Cold air came in low across the dirt and touched the hem of my cloak. The lanterns threw every shadow long enough that the bodies on the ground seemed to reach for the tent wall. No one stepped near them.

I looked once at my sword on the ground, then at the guard. He looked back at me and gave me a little nod.

It lay where I had left it, partly dusted by the guard's boot. I picked up from the ground and used my already dirtied cloak to clean the superficial dirt and slid the bokken to the black cloth belt around my waist.

Jester glanced at me as if she had a question already drawn in her head. Then she looked at the guard and kept it there.

"We are going now?" she asked.

"You are being escorted," the guard said.

"That is like going, but with more people being bossy."

"Jester," Fjord said.

"I know," she said. "I am going very escorted."

Beau snorted once and turned it into a cough before the guard could decide to care.

Caleb moved with Nott tucked close to his side. Fjord watched the guards, then the flap, then the line of people the cover had put around me. He did not ask the question he had the right to ask now. That made him more dangerous than if he had.

At the flap, the Watchmaster's voice followed us.

"Nestled Nook. Until called. No leaving Trostenwald."

The words went out into the night ahead of us.

I stepped after them.

Ahead of me, the name the guard had written beside mine led toward an inn where I had not slept.
