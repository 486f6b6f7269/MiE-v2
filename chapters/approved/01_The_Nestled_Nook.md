# Chapter 01 — The Nestled Nook

The fourth coin had a crack running through the king's nose.

It might not have been a king. The face had a crown, or three points that wanted to be a crown, and a chin worn nearly smooth by other hands. The coin was smaller than the others. Copper, perhaps. I had spent two days learning that people here called metal by colors when they wanted to sound certain about it.

Four coins lay in my palm. Across the room, a woman set down a bowl of stew. A man in a wet coat left something round on the counter. The woman took it without looking at its face.

The bowl went to him.

That was useful. It was also incomplete.

The common room smelled of onions, wool, old smoke, and bread that had been cut while still warm. My stomach had been making practical objections to my plans since sunrise. I had ignored them until the objections acquired a voice.

A low table stood near the hearth, just beyond the traffic from the door. Three people had claimed it without making a show of the claim: a green half-orc with a scarred face and a hand near his cup; a brown-skinned woman in loose blue-gray clothes, sprawled as though the chair had lost an argument with her; and a small blue tiefling with a bright dress and a piece of charcoal moving quickly over a page.

A fourth man had just left their table. I had seen coins move from his hand to theirs. The half-orc had not counted them. The woman had.

I looked back at my own coins.

The innkeeper was pouring something pale and angry into a mug. There was a line of people behind me. Asking the value of a coin was ordinary. Asking it while holding the coins out like evidence of a crime was less ordinary. I could wait for the line to thin. I could watch another transaction. I could leave and remain hungry in a building with no food in it.

None of those options improved while I stood there.

The blue tiefling had stopped drawing.

She was looking at my hand.

Then my stomach made its second objection, louder than the first.

The woman in blue snorted into her drink. The half-orc glanced over. He did not stare. That helped only a little.

The tiefling rose from her chair.

She crossed the room with her sketchbook tucked beneath one arm and the charcoal still in her hand. She stopped at the edge of my table without asking whether I wanted company.

“Are you hungry?” she asked.

The answer had been visible to everyone within six feet. Pretending otherwise now would have made it worse.

“I was attempting to find out whether these buy food.” I opened my hand a little. “The food part has become less theoretical.”

Her eyes went to the coins, then to my face. The left lens of my glasses sat over the eye I kept closed. She did not ask about it. “Oh. Yeah. They probably do. But you look really hungry.”

“I am testing a theory.”

“About whether starving makes money easier?” She leaned closer, studying the coins as if they had offended her personally. “Do you want me to get you something?”

“I have money.”

“You might. But do you want me to pay for breakfast?”

The question was simple. It made too much room around it.

I looked down at the coins in my hand. The edge of one had gone green where somebody else’s thumb had worn it smooth. My stomach made the decision feel urgent. That did not make saying yes easier.

“I…” The word came out and stopped. I tried again. “Yes. Please.”

My face had gone warm anyway.

Her smile softened without becoming smaller. “Okay.” She turned toward the counter and lifted one hand. “Adelaine? Fish stew and bread, please. Fjord says the stew is better than it has any right to be, but Fjord will eat almost anything, so that is not really a review.”

The half-orc made a quiet sound into his cup that might have been disagreement.

It arrived faster than I expected, in a chipped bowl with a heel of dark bread on the rim. I took too large a first bite and had to chew it with the solemn concentration of someone defusing a small bomb.

Jester sat across from me.

The half-orc watched her do it, then watched me. The woman in blue looked amused in the manner of a person who had found an unexpected use for a dull morning.

Jester leaned her elbows on the table. “What’s your name?”

“Mielle.”

“Just Mielle?”

“For the moment.”

Her grin widened. “I’m Jester. Just Jester forever, probably.”

I looked at the charcoal smudge along the side of her hand. “Is that part of the name?”

“The charcoal?”

“The ‘just.’”

She blinked once. Then she laughed, sudden and bright enough that two people near the counter looked over.

“No. But it could be.” She pulled the sketchbook from under her arm. “Do you want me to draw you?”

“No.”

“Okay.” She set the book beside her cup instead of opening it. “I can draw the bread. It looks more cooperative.”

The sausage in the stew was salted hard enough to make my right eye sting. I chose not to examine whether that was the salt.

Jester followed my glance toward the counter. “Those coins are copper. Mostly. I think.”

“Mostly is doing work in that sentence.”

“It is! Money is complicated. You have to know which ones are shiny and which ones are secretly very important.” She lowered her voice, as if sharing a rule of magic. “I’m bad at it too, but Fjord knows things about money sometimes.”

The green half-orc raised his brows at being volunteered.

“Do I?” he asked.

“You know about boats. Boats cost money.”

“That is a chain of reasoning.” He stood, taking his cup with him, and came over only far enough that he could speak without joining the table. His accent had a soft drawl at the edges, practiced or natural; I could not tell, and there was no reason to try. “Those should buy you a meal. Might be worth asking what the meal costs before you put all of them down, though.”

“That was my plan.”

“Then it was a sensible one.”

The woman in blue had turned sideways in her chair. Her eyes went from my cloak to the outline at my left hip, then back to my face.

“You carry a wooden sword around to buy breakfast?” she asked.

I looked down. The cloth tie had slipped under the edge of my cloak when I sat.

“It has not helped yet,” I said.

Jester made a small sound that might have been a laugh swallowed before it could become rude.

The woman smiled with only one corner of her mouth. “What is it for?”

“Practice.”

“Practice at what?”

“Not dropping it.”

That got a real laugh from Jester. The half-orc’s mouth moved as if he had nearly smiled and then decided against confirming it.

“I’m Beau,” the woman said. She had not offered a hand. “That’s Fjord.”

“Mielle.”

“Yeah. We got that far.”

The answer should have annoyed me. Instead, I found myself looking down at the bowl before she could see the beginning of a smile.

The door opened. Cold air came in with two figures who did not belong to the room in the same way the others did.

A thin man in a long, worn coat paused just inside. His hair had been pushed back without much success. Bruising colored one side of his face. Beside and slightly behind him stood a small person in a hood and porcelain mask, holding very still until the innkeeper glanced away.

The thin man chose a table against the wall. The masked person chose the chair with the better view of the door.

A reasonable arrangement. Nothing more could be said from it.

Jester turned in her seat. Curiosity moved through her before the chair had finished scraping the floor.

“Oh,” she said. “Hi.”

The masked person did not answer.

The man looked from Jester to Fjord, then to Beau. His attention passed over me without stopping. It was not an insult. It was a calculation of where a conversation might become dangerous.

“Good morning,” he said.

“Is it?” Beau asked.

He considered that. “It is still occurring.”

Jester laughed again. “I like him.”

The masked person shifted behind his shoulder. The movement exposed green fingers at the edge of one sleeve.

Jester leaned forward. “Are you a goblin?”

Fjord set his cup down. Beau’s posture became more attentive without changing much else. The man in the coat went very still.

The little figure lifted her chin.

“Yes,” she said. Her voice was high and rough at once. “And very dangerous. Extremely dangerous. You should all be frightened.”

I looked at her mask, then at the hand she had tucked close to her chest. It was a good hand for holding a knife, a purse, or someone else’s sleeve. It was also shaking slightly at the wrist.

None of those things told me why.

“I am Mielle,” I said instead.

The masked goblin stared at me.

Then she pointed at my bowl. “Are you going to finish the cheese?”

“Yes.”

“Good. Waste is bad.”

“That seems reasonable.”

Her eyes narrowed, as if I had failed a test whose rules had not been explained.

The man in the coat drew out a chair for her. Not an invitation. A choice placed within reach.

“Caleb,” he said, once she was seated.

“Nott,” said the goblin. “The brave one.”

“Is that a title?” I asked.

“It is earned.”

“Then I will not challenge it before breakfast.”

Nott looked at Caleb. Caleb looked briefly at the ceiling. Jester put both hands over her mouth and failed to hide another laugh.

Jester pointed at Nott’s mask. “Is it hot under there?”

“It is fashionable,” Nott said at once.

Beau leaned back in her chair. “Fashion usually comes with eyeholes that small?”

“So I can see who has the nicest things,” Nott said. “Obviously.”

Fjord looked to Caleb. “You headed somewhere?”

“Eventually,” Caleb said.

“Zadash way?”

Caleb paused a fraction too long. “Perhaps.”

Nott reached for the salt. Caleb moved it closer before she had to stretch for it. “We are very busy,” she added. “Important goblin business.”

“That sounds difficult,” Jester said.

“It is. There is a lot of hiding.”

The conversation did not become easy. It only stopped being one-sided.

A traveler near the job board asked Adelaine how far the Zadash road ran past Trostenwald. She told him, and neither of them was talking to me.

That was the name of the town. I kept it.

Jester looked back at me. “Do you know where you’re going?”

I considered telling the truth. The useful truth was too large. The smaller one was enough.

“Not yet.”

“That’s okay,” she said, immediately. “Sometimes you can find out on the way.”

Fjord glanced at her, then at the road-worn strap of my backpack. “You got a place to stay in town?”

The question was practical. It still had more weight than I wanted to carry across a table.

“For today.”

He accepted the answer for what it was. Beau’s eyes stayed on me a moment longer, but she did not press.

Jester drew a little loaf with wings in the corner of her open sketchbook. I decided not to ask why it had wings.

The door opened for the second time.

The man who entered looked as though color had found him first and then asked whether he wanted more. Lavender skin. Bright clothes layered with symbols and pieces of shining metal. Curled horns hung with small ornaments that made a soft sound when he moved. A tall woman followed him, pale and broad-shouldered under a dark shawl, with hair that faded from black toward white in thick braids.

The room made space for them without being asked.

“Friends,” said the lavender tiefling, spreading his hands. “Potential friends. People who have not yet realized they will enjoy themselves tonight.”

Jester's hand shot up. “Hi!”

The tiefling bowed toward her. “Hello. I am Mollymauk Tealeaf, merchant of delight, reader of cards, occasional moral hazard, and an employee of the Fletching and Moondrop Traveling Carnival of Curiosities.”

He tipped one hand toward the tall woman. “This is Yasha. She does the useful work.”

“Hello,” Yasha said.

“Yasha,” Beau repeated, testing the name.

Yasha gave her a short nod.

“Molly and Yasha,” Jester said, pleased with the names before they had done anything to earn it.

Molly pulled a small deck from inside his coat and let it fan across his fingers. “We have a show tonight. Strange animals, music, fire, things that may be cursed but have signed all the correct paperwork.”

“Have they?” Fjord asked.

“No,” Molly said. “But I admire the concern.”

Nott leaned toward Caleb. “Do they have food?”

Molly turned to her at once. “Of course. We are civilized.”

“Questionable,” Beau said.

“Civilized people are always questionable. That is how you know they have rules.” Molly lifted the deck. “A reading, perhaps? For a silver, I will tell you something you already know in a tone that makes it sound expensive.”

Jester dug in her purse before anyone else could speak. “Me. Do me.”

Molly dealt three cards onto the nearest clear patch of table. His hands were quick, but the cards did not need to be magical to make a room quieter. He turned over one with a painted moon, one with a pair of crossed keys, and one with a little figure holding a lantern much too high.

“The moon,” Molly said, tapping it with one lacquered nail. “You like secrets, especially when they belong to someone else.”

Jester’s smile widened.

“The keys say you see a locked door and think somebody has left you a present.”

“That is true,” Jester said.

“And the lantern says you carry your light high enough for everyone in the room to find you. Which is generous, unless they are trying to hide.”

Jester looked at the cards as though they had told her something very good. “That is also true.”

“See? Worth every silver.” Molly gathered them up. “The rest you will have to see. We begin after dark, east of Lochward on the south bank. Two silver a head. Music, fire, and animals that know more than they should. No refunds if you are bored.”

“Oh, we should go,” Jester said.

Fjord looked toward Molly. “How long does it run?”

“Long enough to earn the money,” Molly said.

Beau set her mug down. “Two silver for weird animals and fire. Fine. I want to see what the paperwork was supposed to cover.”

Nott looked at Caleb. “There will be food.”

“There will be people,” Caleb said.

“There are people here.”

Caleb rubbed his thumb once over the edge of his cup. “We can go. We keep to ourselves.”

“Perfect,” Molly said. “The best kind of guests are the ones who arrive with their own reasons.”

Two silver.

The phrase was precise enough to be useful and useless in equal measure. I looked at the coins I had returned to my pocket. I knew their number. I did not know their relation to silver. I could ask Molly. I could ask the innkeeper. I could wait until everyone had left and inspect the floor for a sign that explained a currency system.

Jester was already looking at me.

“Do you want to go?”

The honest answer arrived too quickly. The word yes was sitting behind my teeth before I had arranged the rest of the sentence.

“I do not know whether I can pay two silver.”

Molly's expression changed only enough to show he had heard the question beneath the words. “A modest obstacle,” he said. “Not an accusation.”

“I have coins,” I said. “I have not yet established whether they are useful coins.”

Jester leaned across the table. “I’ll pay for you.”

The room did not stop. It felt like it had.

“You fed me,” I said.

“Yeah.”

“And now you are buying me a ticket.”

“Yeah.” She smiled, as though the repetition had solved something. “Because I want you to come. You can sit with us. Or near us. Or somewhere else if you want. But you should come because it’ll be fun.”

The offer was small by the standards of people who had money, perhaps. It was also exact. One entry. One evening. No purse passed into my hand. No question attached to it.

I looked at Jester's face. At the charcoal on her fingers. At the little winged loaf in her sketchbook. At the bread crumbs on my plate.

There were ways to refuse kindness until it became a lesson for the person giving it.

I had used those too.

“You are sure?”

Jester’s expression made it clear the question had surprised her. “Yes. I want you to come.”

My throat did something complicated on the way to the answer.

“Then… thank you,” I said. “Just for tonight.”

“Just tonight,” she said, pleased, and did not make me explain the rest.

“Tiny horses,” she added, pointing at me with the charcoal. “They are very serious.”

“Are they?”

“They have tiny hats.”

That was not an answer. It was, however, enough to make me curious.

Molly tucked his cards away. “After dark, then. We shall provide the strange animals and the fire. You lot provide the audience.”

Yasha gave me a brief look as the others began asking about the carnival's location and the time. Her eyes went once to the wooden sword at my hip and then to the closed left lens of my glasses. Nothing in the look asked for an explanation.

“See you tonight,” she said.

The words were plain. I found myself nodding before I had decided whether they deserved an answer.

“I will be there.”

Outside, the morning had stopped being morning without asking my permission. The inn door closed behind me on voices, smoke, and the smell of onions.

I stood beneath the awning with four unfamiliar coins in my pocket, food still warm enough to be a fact inside me, and a place to return to after dark.
