# Chapter 01 — The Nestled Nook

One of the coins had a tower stamped into it, worn almost flat by other hands before mine.

I turned it over twice, then the others. Five in all, none matching, none marked with anything I trusted myself to read as a value. In another life I would have known a currency by weight alone. In this one I had a fistful of dull metal and a stomach that had stopped being polite about it.

A farmer's wife at the counter traded four coins for a loaf and something wrapped in cloth. The woman behind the counter took the coins without checking their faces, which told me the coins were known well enough to trust and told me nothing about what they were worth. Useful. Also incomplete.

I chose a table near the wall instead of the counter, the kind that let me watch a transaction without watching it, and sat with my hands in my lap so they wouldn't do anything without my permission.

Two tables over, three people were finishing something that wasn't food. A man had just left their table; I hadn't seen him arrive, only the coins passing from his hand into theirs. The woman with the shaved sides took the money and counted it into stacks on the wood, unhurried, her lips moving once. The blue-skinned woman beside her, short curled horns, a piece of charcoal in one hand, watched the count without helping it. The man across from them had scars down one side of his face and the stillness of someone who watched doors on principle rather than fear.

I looked away before any of them could decide I'd been staring.

My stomach did not care about my manners and said so again, low and insistent.

The blue-skinned woman had stopped drawing. She was looking at my hand, and then at my face, taking her time with it, the way you take in a puzzle you like. Her attention caught for a moment on my left eye, the closed lid behind the left lens of my glasses, then moved on without deciding anything about it.

She was on her feet before I'd finished working out whether that was a problem.

"Are you okay?"

"I'm fine." In the voice I used when I wasn't.

"You don't look fine. You look hungry." She stopped at the edge of my table, not sitting yet, the charcoal still between two fingers. "I get like that when I draw too long. Do you want food?"

I did want something. The wanting had been loud for an hour. What stopped me was the arithmetic I still had not solved, and the older habit of not taking a thing before I understood what taking it cost.

"I have money," I said. "I'm only not certain what it's worth. I was going to watch someone buy something first."

Jester glanced toward the counter, then back at me. "Do you want me to pay for breakfast?"

The question was simple. It made too much room around it.

I looked down at the coins in my hand. The edge of one had gone green where somebody else's thumb had worn it smooth. My stomach made the decision feel urgent. That did not make saying yes easier.

"I..." The word came out and stopped. I tried again. "Yes. Please."

It was the smallest answer I could give. My face had gone warm anyway.

Jester's smile softened without becoming smaller. "Okay." She turned and lifted a hand. "Adelaine! Fish stew and bread, please. Fjord says the stew is better than it has any right to be, but Fjord will eat almost anything, so that is not really a review."

It arrived faster than I expected, in a chipped bowl with a heel of dark bread on the rim. I took too large a first bite and had to chew it with the concentration of someone defusing something.

Jester dropped onto the bench across from me, chin in one hand, tail curling once behind her like a cat deciding whether to stay. "I'm Jester."

"Mielle."

"Just Mielle?"

"For the moment."

"I'm Jester. Just Jester, probably forever." She said it like the deal had already been struck. "Where are you from? You talk like you learned the language out of a book."

"Somewhere very far from here." Not a lie. Not an answer either, and from the way her eye caught on the gap rather than the words, I saw she'd noticed and decided, for now, to leave it.

"Does your tail always do that," I asked, mostly to have somewhere else to put the question, "or only when you've decided you like someone?"

Jester glanced at her tail, then back at me. "Oh. Both, maybe? That is a very good question." She reached over and stole a piece of my bread without asking, which felt, somehow, like a kind of fairness returned.

The scarred man spoke without turning fully from his own table. "You gonna use those coins, or are you taking Jester's offer?"

"Her offer got there first."

"Fair enough." His vowels rounded in a way that might have been an old habit or a chosen one; there was no telling from one sentence, and no reason to ask. "Fjord."

"Mielle."

"Yeah, we got that far," said the woman with the shaved sides, still not looking up from the coins.

"Beau," Jester said, delighted, as though the introductions were the best part of her morning.

The room changed before the two by the stairs finished coming down it.

A man in a long, worn coat, unshaven, one side of his face still faintly discolored, a bruise that had stopped hurting before it stopped showing. Beside him, small, green-skinned, wrapped in a cloak too large for her, a porcelain mask catching the firelight where her face should have been. One of her hands stayed tucked against her chest, and it was not quite steady.

The man steered her toward a table against the wall, the one with a clear line to the door, and took the chair that put himself between her and the room.

"Good morning," he said, to no one in particular, which is a way of saying it to everyone and giving none of them a handle.

"Is it?" Beau asked.

He considered that. "It is still occurring."

Jester laughed. The masked woman didn't. She had picked the seat that watched the stairs, and she was watching them.

"Is your friend all right?" Jester asked, tipping her head. "She's very quiet."

"She is tired," the man said, in an accent that rounded its vowels differently than Fjord's, differently than mine. He set a hand flat on the table near the small woman's arm, not touching it, marking a small territory around her. "Caleb. We would like to eat in peace."

"Okay. Sorry," Jester said, easily, and did not stop looking, because those were two different promises.

The mask slipped. Not far, an inch when the small woman leaned to set something on the table, but far enough to show a yellow eye and a mouth that was not, by any measure, human. She fixed it back into place with the speed of long practice and glared out from behind it as if daring the room to have seen.

I ate my stew. It was not my table, my history, or a fear I had any standing to manage, and I have been looked at as a thing to be explained often enough to know better than to do it to someone else.

The small woman decided the glare wasn't enough and added words to it. "I'm not shy. I could rob every person in this room and be gone before the door swung." She pointed a green finger at my bowl. "You gonna eat that cheese?"

"Yes."

"Good. Waste is bad."

"I'll consider myself warned."

Her eye narrowed, as though I'd answered a threat with the wrong currency. The man watched the exchange and let it stand. He did not apologize for her or explain her. "Nott," he said, quietly, not quite a correction, and moved the salt within her reach. Then, across the gap between tables, he asked Fjord whether the road east stayed clear this time of year, which was a question about leaving, and everyone at the table understood it as one.

"Depends who's on it," Fjord said. "Zadash way's usually fine."

"Zadash," Caleb repeated, and offered nothing to hang on it.

That was the name of the city. The town's name I got a moment later and sideways: a traveler near the job board asked Adelaine how far the Zadash road ran past Trostenwald, and she told him, and neither of them was talking to me.

Beau had gone back to watching me, or rather my hip, where the cloak sat wrong over whatever was tied under it. "What's the stick."

"A practice sword. Wood. It doesn't do much."

"Didn't ask if it did much. Asked what it was."

I had my own word for it, kept from a life this room was no part of. Bokken wasn't a word this town would know what to do with, and this wasn't the morning to teach it one.

The door opened a second time on a burst of noise arranged to carry. A tall lavender man swept in trailing scarves and the particular confidence of someone who has never once doubted a room would enjoy him, curled horns strung with small ornaments that clicked when he moved. Behind him came a much larger woman, pale, hair fading from black to white in thick braids under a dark blue, tattered shawl, ducking a little under the frame.

"Friends! Potential friends," the lavender man announced, to the room at large. "People who don't know it yet."

"That's a lot to promise a room you just walked into," Jester said, already delighted.

"I get lucky." He set a hand on the larger woman's shoulder and drew her half a step forward, the practiced move of one performer making room for another. "Mollymauk Tealeaf. This is Yasha. She does the useful work."

"Hello," Yasha said. Plain, a little flat, the greeting of someone spending the smallest coin that would settle the debt.

"Yasha," Beau repeated, testing the weight of it, and got a short nod back that ended the matter.

Molly produced a deck from inside his coat and let it fan across his fingers. "Card reading? One copper. I will tell you something true, or at least entertaining."

"Me," Jester said, and had a coin on the table before anyone could take the offer first.

He swept it up, shuffled once, and turned three cards face-up on the wood between them. A painted moon. A pair of crossed keys. A small figure holding a lantern up much too high for its own arm.

"Moon, keys, lantern," Molly said. He tapped the lantern. "You get into places you should not be, then you bring a light with you when you do. Very generous. Very suspicious." He turned the keys toward her. "And you like doors. I think you like the locked ones best."

"That's completely true," Jester said, thrilled, which was the trick of it; he'd said nothing that wasn't true of half the room, and made it feel cut to fit her alone.

"See? Worth every copper." He gathered the cards. "The rest you will have to see. We have a show tonight, east of Lochward on the south bank. Music, fire, animals that know more than they should. Two silver a head. No refunds if you are bored."

"Oh, we should go," Jester said, to the table generally, already deciding for herself and no one else.

Two silver. The number arrived precise and useless at once. I knew how many coins I had. I did not know how they stood against silver, and there was no way to find out that didn't run through saying so out loud.

"I don't have the money for it," I said, to Jester rather than the room. Easier to say to one person than to seven.

"Oh." She blinked, recalculated, brightened. "I'll get you in."

I looked at the offer a beat too long. A meal was one thing, small enough to swallow. This had a gate and a price on it, and a person deciding again that I was worth the spending.

"You already bought me breakfast," I said. "You don't have to—"

"I know I don't have to." She was already reaching for her purse. "But I want you to go. We got paid this morning, me and Fjord and Beau. We helped a man with his daughter, and he gave us money. I have enough."

"You did help his daughter," I said. "I did nothing to earn a share of that."

"You don't have to earn a carnival." She said it like it was obvious, which for her it was.

There are ways to refuse a kindness until refusing it becomes the unkind thing. I had used most of them, in lives this one knew nothing about. My throat did something complicated on the way to the answer.

"Then thank you," I said, and had to look at the table to say it. "One evening. That's all I'll take from it."

"One evening," she agreed, pleased, and did not make me hold to the second half of that out loud.

Beau snorted into her mug. Fjord looked to Molly. "What time?"

"After dark," Molly said. "You will not miss it."

At the wall table, Caleb said something low, and Nott answered louder than she meant to, "I want to see the animals," and then looked annoyed at having wanted anything out loud.

"We'll come," Caleb said. "But we keep to ourselves."

"The best kind of guest," Molly said, and meant it or performed it well enough that the difference didn't matter yet.

Yasha's attention passed over me once on the way out, catching for a moment on the closed lens and the sword at my hip, and moving on the way you set down something you have decided isn't yours to pick up. She didn't ask. I was grateful, in a small way I didn't examine, that no one at the table had.

Outside, the coins were still in my pocket, five of them, no closer to a value. The stew sat warm and factual under my ribs. Somewhere south of Lochward there was a field with a gate I now had a reason to walk through after dark, and north of here there was a broken building nobody in that room knew about, and I meant to keep both those facts where they were.

I pulled the cloak straight over the sword and went to find the second one before the first one opened.
