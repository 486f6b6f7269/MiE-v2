# Chapter 05 — Temporary address

The guard behind me had a loose buckle.

It clicked against his breastplate every fourth step, then every third when the street tilted downward. I listened to it because the alternative was listening to the silence walking with us.

Trostenwald had closed around the carnival. Shutters covered most of the windows. A few lamps remained behind glass, too high and too far inside to warm the street. Where people had gathered at the corners, the guards sent them back with a look or a lifted hand. No one asked what had happened beneath the striped canvas. They looked at the dried blood on us and decided they could wait.

Jester walked on my right.

She had been beside my left inside the tent. I noticed the change after half a street and then disliked myself for noticing it. There were several ordinary reasons. The guards had arranged us badly. Fjord had taken the outer edge. Beau kept moving ahead and falling back whenever the pace irritated her. Caleb and Nott had become one narrow piece of darkness behind us.

Jester was simply walking.

Her sleeve brushed mine once when we avoided a cart wheel left against a wall.

"Are you cold?" she whispered.

The guard with the loose buckle looked at us.

"No," I said.

I was cold. It was not currently the largest problem.

Jester glanced at the clasp of my cloak, then at my face. She opened her mouth. Fjord looked over his shoulder.

She closed it again.

This was apparently what restraint looked like on Jester. It seemed painful.

The street narrowed near a cooper's shop. A post leaned out from the covered front at exactly the height of my sword. I saw it. I adjusted for it. The black cloth at my waist caught the edge anyway, and the bokken struck the post with a hollow knock loud enough to turn three heads.

I stopped before the belt could pull me backward.

The guard behind me did not.

His breastplate touched my backpack. I took a short step forward, caught my cloak under my heel, and spent one graceless second deciding which part of myself to rescue first.

Beau's hand closed around my elbow before the street did.

"Careful," she said.

It sounded like an accusation.

"I had identified the post."

"Yeah. Looked personal."

She released me as soon as I had both feet beneath me. Her eyes dropped to the wooden sword and stayed there for half a beat. She had watched it break limbs inside the tent. The post had survived without difficulty.

Nott made a small noise from behind Caleb's coat.

"Maybe it is only dangerous when she wants it to be."

"That would be preferable," Caleb murmured.

Fjord looked at the guard, judged that no one intended to stop us over a defeated piece of wood, and turned forward again.

Jester leaned closer.

"I do that with doors," she whispered. "Not the sword thing. I don't have a sword. But sometimes the door is very clearly there and then it attacks my shoulder."

I looked at her.

She was smiling, but not at me. Not exactly. She had put the embarrassment between us and made herself stand beside it.

"Do the doors apologize?" I asked.

"Never. Doors are assholes."

The guard's buckle clicked.

I faced forward before my mouth could do anything disloyal.

The Nestled Nook appeared at the end of the street with two lamps burning over its sign. I knew the entrance. I knew the table where Jester had put food in front of me after asking first. I knew the stairs led somewhere above, because Caleb and Nott had come down them that morning. The rest of the building belonged to people who had paid for it.

The guards brought us to the door as if delivering a parcel they expected to find unopened later.

One of them entered first. The woman behind the counter looked up from a cup and then looked past him at all of us. Her eyes moved over blood, dirt, weapons, Nott's mask, my cloak, and Beau's expression. Whatever question she had chosen did not survive the guard's face.

"These people are staying here," he said. "They are not to leave Trostenwald until the Watchmaster calls for them. If they try, send for the Crownsguard."

The woman set her cup down.

"All of them?"

"All of them."

Her gaze paused on me.

I stood very still. This had not helped at the carnival. I tried it again anyway.

Fjord stepped half a pace forward. "We understand the order."

"You said that before," the guard replied.

"Still true."

The guard looked as though he might have preferred an argument. Fjord did not provide one.

Behind him, the second guard rested a hand on the door and looked at Caleb and Nott. Caleb kept his chin lowered. Nott had become almost entirely cloak.

"Until called," the first guard said. "No trips outside the walls. No disappearing before dawn."

"We are very tired," Jester said. "Disappearing sounds like too much work."

"Jester," Fjord said.

"I am agreeing."

The guard studied her, failed to discover whether she had agreed, and left.

The loose buckle went with him. Its clicking crossed the threshold, moved down the street, and finally stopped being the loudest thing I could hear.

The door shut.

No one spoke.

The woman behind the counter looked from Fjord to Beau. "I am going to assume the blood is not from a disagreement over supper."

"Carnival," Beau said.

The woman waited.

"It was a bad carnival."

"I gathered that."

Fjord rubbed one hand over his face. "We can explain what we're allowed to explain in the morning."

"You can explain why the Crownsguard believes six people are staying under my roof when I have keys out for five."

Six.

The count entered the air and found me immediately.

Jester turned before anyone else did.

"Where have you actually been staying?"

There was no accusation in it. No careful opening. She asked the way she had asked what I wanted to eat, whether I knew the carnival, and if the closed side of my face hurt. The question arrived whole because it had occurred to her whole.

Heat climbed from my collar to my ears.

I could have said elsewhere.

I could have said I had an arrangement. That was close enough to the answer I had given the Watchmaster. It was also a phrase built to end conversation, and Jester had asked without building any trap around it.

Her hands were empty. She did not reach for me. She only waited.

"An abandoned building," I said.

Jester blinked.

The woman behind the counter stopped reaching for her cup.

I adjusted my glasses, though they had not moved.

"For the last two days," I added. The second fact was worse. It made the first one real enough to count nights inside it.

Jester's mouth opened.

Beau spoke first.

"You were sleeping in a condemned building?"

"I do not know whether it was condemned."

"Did it have a roof?"

"Most of one."

Beau stared at me.

"That is not the defense you think it is."

"I was not defending it."

"Good, because the building is losing."

Fjord's eyes had gone narrow in the practical way they did when a problem refused to stay small.

"Was it inside town?"

"Yes."

"Could the guard find you there?"

I considered lying. Not because I wanted to deceive him. Because the answer was embarrassing in a different direction.

"Probably not."

He breathed out through his nose.

"All right. Then the address in that ledger needs to be true tonight."

He did not ask which building. He did not ask how I had entered it. The omission was deliberate enough that I could feel its edges.

Caleb stood near the foot of the stairs with one hand resting lightly against Nott's shoulder. His eyes moved once over my cloak, the backpack, the dirt still caught at the hem.

"It was dry?" he asked.

"In parts."

He nodded as if I had answered a different question too.

Nott looked around his coat.

"Were there rats?"

"One."

"Only one?"

"Only one that objected to me."

"That means there were more."

"That was also my conclusion."

Nott's head tilted. "Did you name it?"

"No."

"That is why it objected."

Caleb's hand pressed once against her shoulder. Nott retreated by several inches, though not entirely.

Jester had not moved.

The brightness in her face had changed. It had not vanished. It had become very focused.

"You slept there for two nights?"

"Yes."

"And you didn't have food."

"Not enough to mention."

"You mentioned it by almost falling over."

My face grew hotter.

"That was not intentional."

"I know."

She said it softly enough that the answer did not become another joke for everyone to hold.

Beau looked toward the counter. "How much for another bed?"

The woman named a number.

It meant nothing to me. I knew it was a number. I knew Beau's expression sharpened slightly and Fjord's did not. I knew Jester did not look at either of them.

She looked at me.

"You can stay with me."

The sentence went through every defense I had prepared and found none of them facing the right direction.

Jester continued before I could answer.

"Just tonight. I have space, and you are small, and I don't kick very much unless I have a really exciting dream. Fjord says I kick, but he was in the other bed and therefore cannot be considered an unbiased witness."

"You kicked the wall," Fjord said.

"The wall was very close to the bed."

"It was a wall."

"Exactly. Suspicious."

The woman behind the counter picked up her cup and decided not to participate.

I put one hand into the side of my backpack. The four coins were wrapped in a piece of cloth near the top. I knew where they were by touch.

Jester watched my hand disappear.

"What are you doing?"

"I have money."

"You had money this morning."

"I still have it. This is generally considered a feature of not spending it."

Beau made a sound that might have been a laugh and then covered it with her knuckles.

I brought out the cloth and unfolded it in my palm.

Four coins. Still small. Still capable of being worth almost anything to someone who knew what they were.

"I do not know whether this is enough," I said. "Or too much. Or currency from somewhere that would make the innkeeper summon another guard. But I can contribute."

Jester leaned over my hand.

"That one is a copper. That one too. That one is also a copper."

She touched the last coin with one blue fingertip.

"And this one is a button."

I looked down.

The last piece had a hole through its center.

It had possessed the same confidence as the other three.

"I see."

"Do you?" Beau asked.

"I see it now."

Nott slipped out from behind Caleb far enough to examine the evidence.

"It is a nice button."

"Thank you."

"I might steal it."

"Nott," Caleb said.

"I said might. That is honesty."

I closed the cloth over the coins and button.

Jester put her hand over mine before I could return them to the backpack. Not gripping. Just stopping the motion for one second.

"You don't have to pay me."

My hand forgot what it had been doing.

Her palm was warm. There was dried dirt along the side of one finger and a faint smear of dark blood near her wrist that neither of us had noticed until then.

I wanted to be her friend.

The thought arrived without permission and with none of the useful instructions that usually came with a fact. It did not tell me how to become one. It did not explain whether she wanted the same thing. It only made the space beneath her hand harder to leave.

"I do not want to become an obligation," I said.

The sentence came out too formal. That happened when the simpler sentence was too exposed.

Jester frowned.

"You're not an obligation. You're Mielle."

There were several objections available. They all failed for the same reason: she had not said it as philosophy. She had said it because those were the two categories in front of her and she thought I had put myself in the wrong one.

Fjord shifted his weight near the stairs.

"Staying where the Watch knows to find you is the safest choice for tonight," he said. "Tomorrow can remain tomorrow."

He did not say we. He did not say group. He gave the night a boundary and left the rest outside it.

Beau folded her arms.

"And the abandoned building can survive one evening without you defending its honor."

"Its honor was already limited."

"Good. Progress."

Caleb looked down at Nott. She looked back at him through the mask.

"We should go upstairs," he said.

"Because this is private?"

"Because it is late."

"It can be two things."

"Ja."

Nott pointed one small finger at me before following him.

"Keep the button hidden."

"I will take precautions."

"Good. There are thieves here."

Caleb guided her toward the stairs without looking at any particular thief.

Fjord waited until they had passed, then gave Jester a look that contained at least three practical warnings and one tired acceptance. Jester smiled at him with complete innocence.

He did not believe it.

"One night," he said.

"That is what I said."

"I'm making sure you heard yourself."

"I hear myself all the time."

"That's what worries me."

He went upstairs.

Beau followed more slowly. At the first step she looked back at me.

"If the building collapses while you're gone, it wasn't because you abandoned it emotionally."

"I will try to endure the guilt."

"There you go."

She disappeared after Fjord.

The woman at the counter lifted her cup toward Jester. "No fire. No blood on the blankets. If either of you breaks furniture, I charge the blue one."

"That is discrimination," Jester said.

"You paid for the room."

"Oh. That is fair."

She released my hand.

The absence remained warm for several seconds.

"So," she said, and turned toward the stairs. "Do you need anything from the abandoned building?"

"No."

The answer came too quickly.

Jester looked back.

I adjusted it.

"Nothing that cannot remain there until the guards decide we are allowed to move freely."

"Okay. Do you have sleeping clothes?"

The Perfect Wardrobe could solve that. The backpack could provide whatever I selected if I reached into it carefully. Both answers were possible. Neither needed explanation in the front of an inn after midnight.

"Yes."

"Do you snore?"

"I do not know."

"That's exciting."

"Is it?"

"No. But it could be funny."

She started up the stairs.

I followed.

The stair was narrow enough that the bokken brushed the railing on the first turn. I caught it before it struck hard. Jester looked over her shoulder.

"The stairs are attacking you now."

"They have allied with the doors."

"I knew it."

Her delight made the failure worse and easier at the same time.

At the upper landing, the corridor divided around a support beam. Caleb and Nott had already vanished behind one of the closed doors. Fjord's voice carried faintly from farther down, followed by Beau telling him she was not going to discuss anything until she had washed carnival off her arms.

Jester stopped at a door near the end.

Her hand rested on the latch.

"You can take the bed if you want," she said. "Or we can both take parts of it. Or I can make a blanket place on the floor, but you are not sleeping on the floor because you already did the abandoned-building version of that and it sounded bad."

"I can take the floor."

"No."

The answer was immediate.

I stared at the latch instead of her face.

"A portion of the bed would be acceptable," I said. "Provided your kicking remains within the limits you described."

"I described no limits."

"That is true."

She opened the door.

Warm lamplight reached the corridor. A dress lay across the back of a chair. A travel bag sat open near the wall with paper, string, and the corner of a sketchbook visible inside. The bed was not large. It was also dry, intact, and located in a building where the roof had made a complete commitment.

Jester stepped aside.

She did not pull me in. She did not repeat the offer. She left enough space for me to decide again now that the threshold was real.

My hand found the strap of my backpack. The bokken rested against my hip. Three copper coins and one button waited inside a piece of cloth.

I stepped past her.

"One night," I said.

"One night," Jester agreed.

The door closed behind us.
