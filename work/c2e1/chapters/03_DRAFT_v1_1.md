# Chapter 03 — The curious show

The first note had nowhere to go.

It rose from Toya's small body near the far side of the tent and found the ropes above us, the lanterns, the damp canvas, the breath held in the people around me. By the time it returned, it had acquired the next note. Then another.

I had not known singing could alter the size of a room.

The child beside the aisle stopped worrying the paper animal in his hands. Jester had gone still in a way I had not yet seen from her. Even Beau, who had spent the first part of the performance looking as though she had agreed to be inconvenienced by it, had stopped making faces at the ceiling.

Toya sang about a river. I could not have repeated the words afterward. The sound was enough without them.

Jester leaned a little toward me from the center of the front seats. “She is good, right?”

“She appears to be winning,” I said.

Jester's smile started before the song took it away again.

The old man stood.

At first I thought he had decided he needed the exit. His head bowed. One hand closed over his chest. The people nearest him shifted aside with the polite impatience reserved for someone who had chosen the wrong moment to become ill.

Then his fingers bent farther than fingers should.

The song broke in the middle of a note.

Toya made a noise too small to be called a scream. Kylre was beside her before the sound had finished, lifting her from the boards and carrying her through the flap behind the performers. Gustav shouted for people to leave. Bo was already moving toward the entrance, trying to make the words mean something against the first rush of bodies.

The old man lifted his head.

There was no old man left in the movement that followed.

His back had opened into the wrong posture. His mouth was too wide. The front row folded away from him, chairs scraping and knees striking one another, but the people behind them had not yet understood why they were being pushed.

Jester was the first person near me to act.

A sharp blue-white flare crossed the space between her hand and the thing that had been a man. Another Jester appeared beside the first, close enough to make my eye want to correct the count. The creature flinched toward the light.

Then it saw the woman trying to run.

She was not far from it. That was the difficulty.

She ducked under its first reach. Someone struck her from behind in the crush. Her foot slipped on loose dirt. The creature's second blow landed before she had finished falling.

I stood.

There was a route to her in the abstract. It ran through a man dragging his daughter by the wrist, a woman on her knees beside an overturned bench, two people trying to pass each other through the same narrow space, and the bodies still forcing toward the entrance. It did not exist in the tent.

The woman stopped moving.

For one moment, the thing that had killed her looked beyond her body for another person to take.

I stepped into the gap it had made.

“Mielle?” Jester said.

I did not answer. There was nothing useful to say.

My left hand was empty until it was not.

The bokken settled into my palm with the ordinary weight it had always had. The cloth tie was still around it. A few people saw it happen. I knew because the sound of the crowd changed in the small space around me, not louder, only broken by breaths that had stopped at different times.

Yasha had been passing weapons back as fast as hands could take them. Her head turned toward the pile beside her. There was an empty place where the pale length of wood had been.

The creature came for me.

It did not feint. Its shoulder drove forward. Its weight settled hard over one leg, and its arm swung down in a line that would have caught my head if I had remained where I was.

I moved one step to the side.

The air changed against my cheek as its hand went past.

The bokken struck the outside of its knee.

The impact should have carried bone and balance with it. Instead, the wood met something dense and wrong beneath the skin. The creature stumbled half a step, more surprised than hurt, and turned back toward me with the same unfinished hunger.

Harder than a person.

Behind it, the woman on the ground drew in a breath.

It was not a living breath.

Her body rose on hands that did not seem to remember the work. Her head came up too slowly. When she looked at me, there was no recognition in her face.

The second creature crossed the space between us in a rush.

Its hand came low for my stomach. The first creature came high from the other side, recovering its balance and reaching with the arm I had not touched.

I let the second hand pass in front of me and took one short step back around the first creature's shoulder line. The two bodies nearly struck each other. The woman's fingers closed on empty air. The old man's hand cut through the space where my collarbone had been.

Neither reached me.

The black began at the grip of the bokken.

It moved over the wood without light, darkening the grain until the weapon looked lacquered in a color that did not belong to the tent. The first creature tried to turn with me. Its arm came up, broad and careless, to force me away from the bodies behind it.

I met the forearm as it descended.

The sound was smaller than I expected.

Its arm bent under the strike at the middle rather than the elbow. The creature made a wet, furious noise and pulled the broken limb close without understanding why it no longer answered correctly.

A crossbow bolt passed over my shoulder and struck the canvas beyond it.

“Sorry!” Nott called from somewhere behind Caleb. Her voice was high with terror and offense. “It moved!”

“It is allowed to move,” Caleb said.

A thin line of fire followed the bolt, close enough to make the old man's remaining arm recoil. Caleb had found a place behind a support where the crowd could not reach him. Nott crouched lower beside him, loading another bolt with hands that shook only until they needed to work.

Fjord had his falchion back. He did not shout an order. He watched the two creatures, the bodies still fleeing behind them, and the narrow space I had taken between both problems.

“Keep them turned,” he said.

It was not addressed to me alone. It was simply the useful thing in the room.

The old man tried to answer with his shoulder instead of his broken arm. I moved inside the reach before the mass of him could settle. The black bokken came down across the leg that carried him.

This time the sound did not remain small.

His knee held. The lower part of the leg did not. He folded to one side, caught himself with the arm that still worked, and tried to drag himself forward anyway.

The woman-creature chose the opening. She came around him toward a boy who had fallen near the edge of the retreating crowd.

Molly was moving toward the boy at the same time, one blade in each hand and his bright coat looking impossible beside the dirt and frightened people. He did not reach the child first.

I did.

The woman-creature's arm swept for the ground. I stepped between the line of it and the boy, not touching him, only changing the place where her hand had to go. The strike missed my ribs by less than the width of the bokken.

I turned with it.

The black wood struck the length of her arm as it passed.

She did not fall. The arm simply ceased to be shaped for striking. Her hand dragged behind her when she tried to turn it back toward me.

Molly reached the boy and lifted him under one arm, carrying him toward the performers' side without asking whether the boy wished to be carried. The decision appeared sound.

“Gustav!” he called. “This way.”

Gustav answered from the flap with a voice that had become raw from using it.

Jester's duplicate darted closer to the woman-creature. The real Jester stood with one hand raised, her face too pale beneath the blue. A wash of color crossed the ground between them. The creature recoiled from it, then turned toward Jester instead.

I had no time to consider whether that was fear, pain, or confusion. The old man had pulled himself around on three limbs and was still moving. The woman-creature had one broken arm and a clear line toward Jester.

Beau came through that line first.

She had her staff back. It struck the woman-creature across the shoulder, hard enough to shift the whole body sideways, then Beau was already past the return swing. She did not stay where the creature could close both hands around her. She hit, moved, watched its feet, and hit again when it followed the wrong person.

“Gross,” Beau said.

It was not clear whether she meant the creature or the situation.

Yasha reached the old man with her greatsword in both hands. Her face had changed less than everyone else's. That made it worse.

The creature reached for her with its good arm.

Yasha took the reach on the flat of her blade, stepped into the contact, and put the sword through the space it had opened for her.

The old man stopped trying to move.

For a moment, the tent had only one creature left.

The woman-creature looked at the body on the ground. Then at Yasha. Then at Beau. Its good leg drew beneath it.

It wanted distance. It wanted a person past the line of people who could hurt it.

Fjord saw the same thing. He moved left with the falchion low, not chasing it, only making the entrance route narrower. Nott's second bolt struck the creature's side. Caleb's fire caught the dirt at its feet and made it change direction again.

It chose me.

That was acceptable.

It came forward with the leg that still worked and the arm that still could close. I gave it the center of the space for one step, then took it back when the shoulder committed. The bokken met the outside of its lower leg.

The black surface cracked the limb beneath it.

The creature pitched forward. Beau was already there.

She stepped over the broken line of its body, drove the end of the staff down, and did not let it rise again.

The sound after that was not silence. It was people remembering they had voices.

Somebody was crying near the entrance. Somebody else was being sick. The lanterns still hung in their rings above us. One of them turned slowly on its hook, as though the tent had not noticed it had stopped being a tent for a show.

The Crownsguard reached us after the creatures were down.

They came with weapons drawn and expressions that made it clear they had seen enough to be frightened but not enough to know what they feared. One guard pointed at Yasha first. Another pointed at Beau. A third saw the bokken in my hand and paused.

“Put the weapons down,” he said.

I lowered the bokken and laid it on the dirt beside my foot.

Jester was closer than I had noticed. Her duplicate was gone.

“You are not hurt,” she said.

“No.”

“You were in front of both of them.”

“They were facing me.”

“That is what I said.”

A guard stepped between us before I found an answer that did not sound wrong.

“No one leaves,” he said. “Not the carnival. Not any of you. You will remain in Trostenwald until this is sorted.”

Molly stood near Gustav with one blade lowered and the other still dark at the edge. Caleb had one hand near Nott's shoulder without quite touching it. Fjord watched the guards instead of the bodies. Beau leaned on her staff, breathing hard through her nose. Yasha looked down at the bokken on the dirt for half a second, then back at the guard who had ordered it down.

The black had left the wood.

It was a plain piece of pale timber again, lying between the shoe of a Crownsguard and the mark where the woman-creature's broken leg had struck the ground.
