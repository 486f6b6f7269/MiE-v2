# Chapter 03 — The curious show

The girl on the stage had a voice too large for her.

She could not have been more than twelve. She stood alone in the ring of lantern light with her hands folded, and the sound that came out of her did not match the small careful person it came from. It filled the tent to the canvas and settled over the crowd like weather. The child two seats down had stopped folding the paper animal. The irritated cough behind the performers' flap had gone quiet. Even Beau, who had spent the whole wait making remarks at the back of Fjord's head, had gone still.

I let it reach me. There was no reason not to. A held tent, a held breath, a girl singing better than a road town had any right to hear: it asked for nothing but attention, and I gave it, and some tired place behind my ribs loosened a degree in the giving. I did not look for a threat in it. Nothing in the room told me to.

That is the part I keep returning to. Nothing told me to.

An old man rose from the seats inward and to my left, nearer the center pole than I was. I noticed him only because he stood during the song, and standing during the song was wrong. He took a step toward the light. Then his back bent the wrong way.

It was not a stagger. Bones do not stagger. Something under his coat lengthened, and the coat tore along the seam of it, and the sound the crowd had been holding broke all at once into the other kind of sound.

The song stopped. Across the tent a man's voice went up over the noise, older, carrying, the voice of someone used to being obeyed by a work crew: get them out, back doors, move. Gustav, though I did not know his name yet. A wiry man in performer's motley took the singing girl around the waist and swung her bodily off the back of the stage and through the performers' flap, and both of them were gone before I had finished understanding that the thing in the middle of the ring was no longer a man.

Then everyone was standing, and the standing became a wall.

Jester was on her feet with a hand already thrown out. I did not see the whole of what she did, only the flare of it, a hard flat crack of light against the creature's face that rocked its head sideways, and then a second Jester standing where no Jester had been, close and grinning and wrong, drawing its eye. The real one was already moving. That was hers, all of it, before I had left my seat.

The creature swung at the false grin, met nothing, and turned. Between it and the crowd was a young woman who had gotten only two rows before the crush stopped her.

She ducked the first blow. She was fast, and lucky, and it went over her as she dropped. She did not get up in time for the second.

I want to be exact about this, because I have been dishonest with myself about smaller things and I will not be about this one. I was at the outer edge. She was inward of me, past the old man's empty seat, past a fold of bodies already collapsing into the gap between us. Between her and my hands there was no line that did not run through three other people at a dead run. I could have reached her by knocking them down. I could not have reached her before the second blow by any route that existed. I looked, and the looking took less time than it takes to read this, and there was nothing at the end of it.

She fell and did not move, and the creature lifted its head to find the next one.

The next one was going to be a child, or the woman with two children, or the man still fighting the entrance flap with too many people behind him. It was going to be whoever the thing could reach, and it could reach into the crowd faster than the crowd could clear.

So I stopped being at the edge.

I did not run. Running through those people would have made another falling body, and I had just watched what a falling body was worth here. I walked into the pocket of cleared ground the creature made around itself by being what it was, and I walked with my hands empty, because the bokken was across the tent in a pile beside Yasha's boot.

It was in my hand before I reached the middle.

I called it and it came, the way it always comes, out of nowhere I can point to and into my grip as if it had never been anywhere else. The tie of it settled against my palm. I did not look to see who saw. Later I would learn that Yasha had seen, because Yasha was standing over the weapons and a wooden sword does not leave a pile on its own, but in the moment I had a creature deciding what to make of me.

I know what I look like. A small girl, a head shorter than anyone worth fearing, one eye shut behind a cracked-looking lens, a red hood, and a stick of practice wood held in two hands like someone who had been told which end to point. I have spent a life being the least dangerous thing in every room I enter, and I have learned not to correct the impression until it costs someone to have held it.

It cost this one.

It came at me low and committed, all its new mass behind one shoulder, sure of its size. And a half-breath before the reach became a lunge, I knew the line of it. Not saw. Knew, the way you know a held note is about to break, a warning that arrives just ahead of the motion and gives it away: the vector, the timing, the exact stretch of ground its claw meant to own. That much I have. It is not sight of the future and it is not the creature's mind and it did not tell me one thing about why an old man had become this. It told me where the next second was going to happen, and no more, and I still had to put my own feet somewhere it wasn't.

I gave the line one short step of distance and let the mass go past me, and while its outside leg was still planted I brought the bokken down on the knee.

Wood on it should have cracked. Wood on it did not. The shock ran back up my arms as if I had struck a fence post sunk in stone. Harder than a man, much harder, under a skin that only looked like skin. It went off the planted leg all the same, because even a fence post can be knocked off its base, and the stagger bought the man at the entrance the two seconds he needed to shove through.

Behind me Yasha had already turned to the pile. I heard it more than saw it: her voice, flat and short, throwing names and steel. Fjord's falchion went to him underhand and he caught it without looking. Beau's staff. Caleb's coat-flash as something small came back to his hand. The weapons were coming home. I only had to keep this thing's eye on me while they did.

It came again, angrier for the leg, and this time I did not strike to knock it off balance. This time I struck to break something, and when the bokken met the arm it swung at me the wood was not only wood. Black ran down the length of it and set hard over the surface, a second skin laid on in the half-second of the swing, and the arm that should have batted a toy aside broke across it instead. Not because I found a secret joint. Because I put a reinforced edge into a limb the creature had left open and drove it, and the limb was harder than a man and I was harder than the limb for exactly as long as it took. It shrieked out of something not shaped for shrieking and hauled the ruin of the arm back against its chest.

Then the dead woman stood up.

She rose the way the old man had risen, wrong, her spine finding an angle a spine should not find, and the sound in the tent changed again. I understood nothing about why. I had no theory of it, and no second to spend building one. A woman had been killed and now the killing was on its feet, and whether the first creature had done it to her or the song had or the night itself had, I did not know.

Two of them now. One with a broken arm. One new and whole, turning its head to learn the room.

I moved. Not far, and never in a straight line, because a straight line lets two things reach you from two sides. I kept them both in front of me the way you keep two dogs in front of you, never giving either one my back, letting the ground and the center pole and the fallen do half the work of the wall. The new one came at me first, greedy, sure of its size the way the first had been, and it had not watched me long enough to learn better. Its lunge announced itself the same half-beat early. I stepped off the line, and its own reach carried its arm out straight and open across my body, and I brought the black-edged wood down on it in passing. That arm broke the way the first one's had.

And that was when the others reached the line, and the fight stopped being only mine.

Fjord came in on my right with the falchion and a grunt of breath, taking an angle that let him open the first creature's flank without crossing into where I was working; the cut landed heavy and the thing turned into it, which turned it away from a knot of people at the entrance. Caleb had put his back near the pole with Nott folded in at his side, and he spoke three hard syllables and threw his hand forward, and a line of fire went off the newer creature's shoulder with a sound like wet cloth tearing and a stink that reached me six feet away. Nott's crossbow spoke twice from the shelter of his coat, once into the dirt, once into the burned shoulder, and the creature flinched off the second bolt onto its bad side. Molly was somewhere to my left, bright and loud, herding the last of the aisle toward the flap in a voice pitched to carry over panic, and then the voice cut out because a creature had gotten close enough that herding was no longer the job, and his two curved swords were out and painting it with shallow red lines to keep it off the people at his back.

I did not command any of it. There was no room to and it was not offered. Each of them chose a place, and I fought in the room they left me.

The newer creature, burned and bolt-stung, wheeled off Caleb's fire and onto the leg Nott had turned it toward, and the turn put its whole weight over that leg at a bad angle. That was the opening, and it was theirs as much as mine; I only spent it. I stepped in behind its own stumble and took the leg at the knee with the reinforced wood while it was already falling toward the fault, and it went down onto the dirt with a broken arm and now a broken leg to match, dragging itself half-upright on what it had left.

An arm and a leg on each. Through the openings the fight gave me, and two of those openings the others made. I stopped there. I did not reach for more, and no part of me wanted to be the one to finish it, and I was not alone in the room, so I did not have to be.

Yasha came for the first creature, last and largest. The greatsword was already up when she reached it, and the rage was on her the way weather is on a sky. The thing was down on a broken arm and a broken leg by then, and none of that slowed her hand. She stepped over the wreck of it, brought the sword down through it, and it stopped being anything at all.

The second was still quick even ruined, and it threw itself off its good side at a man who had frozen against the stage, and Beau met it. She came off a run low and fast, into the side I had kept turned toward the open floor, her whole body behind the strike. What she did to it I felt in my own teeth. It folded and did not get up.

Then it was quiet, in the way a room is quiet when it has just been very loud. A child was crying somewhere. Someone else was saying a name over and over and getting no answer. The lanterns still burned. The center pole still held the roof up. On the dirt between the pole and the stage lay the two of them, the old man and the young woman, their own bodies mostly returned to them now that whatever had worn them had let go. An hour ago one had been queuing for a card reading and the other had been two rows from the door. The blood on the ground did not say which was which.

I lowered the bokken. My hands were steady, which felt like a small obscene thing, so I stopped looking at them.

The guards came in through the entrance while the crowd was still draining out of it, and they came hard, shields first, vermilion and dull bronze, crossbows up and swords loose. They shouted for stillness, for weapons down, for everyone to hold. A man built like a doorway took one look at the middle of the tent, at the swords out and the bodies down and the small red-hooded girl standing over the wreck of it with a wooden sword, and I watched him decide that no one was leaving Trostenwald tonight.

I crouched and set the bokken flat on the dirt and lifted my empty hand where he could see it. Losing it did not frighten me; I could call it back from the bottom of a river if it came to that. What mattered was that a frightened man with a crossbow saw a small girl put a weapon down and step back from it without being told twice.

His eyes went to the wooden sword on the ground, took the measure of it, and moved on. A stick, painted black along one edge with something he had no time to wonder about, in the hands of a girl half a head shorter than his crossbow. Whatever had opened two people from the inside out, it had not been that. He was already past it, past me, to the swords still bare in the hands of the people who had actually cut these things down.

"Weapons on the ground," he said, to the tent. "All of them. Now." Fjord's falchion went down. Beau's staff. Yasha stood a moment longer than the others before the greatsword touched the dirt, and the sergeant marked her for it, the big pale woman who had put a blade through one of the bodies, and I watched him decide she was the account he would want first.

The whole tent was a sum that did not add up, and he looked at it the way you look at one you have decided to carry to someone paid to solve sums. He was not going to pretend he understood any of it, and he was not going to let it walk out the flap while he didn't.

"Nobody leaves," he said, to me and past me, to the tent. "Not the town, not till the Crown says. Somebody bring me whoever runs this circus." Then he was already turning to the living and the dead and the work of telling them apart.

Jester had come up on my left without my hearing her, which people manage more often than I would like. She looked at the bokken on the ground, then at me, and her eyes caught for a second on the shut side of my face where the lantern found the closed lid behind my glasses. Whatever question moved behind her own eyes, she left it where it was.

"You walked at it," she said. Quiet, for her. "With nothing in your hands."

"It had its hands full of the crowd." I did not look at her while I said it. "I only needed them full of me instead."

She thought about that. Behind us the guards were making one line of the living and a separate reckoning of the dead, and the flap stayed shut against the night, and no one moved toward it.

"That's a terrible reason," Jester said.

"It worked."

"It's still terrible." But she said it looking at the closed side of my face and not away from it, the way most people looked away.

She stayed where she was, on my left, at the edge. She did not fill the quiet with anything, which I had not expected of her and was grateful for. Across the tent a guard called a number to another guard, counting the dead. The flap stayed shut against the night. Neither of us moved toward it, and neither of us said why.
