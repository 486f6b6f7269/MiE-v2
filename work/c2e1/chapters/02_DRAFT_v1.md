# Chapter 02 — Before the gates

The fourth coin still had a crack through the face.

I had turned it over often enough that the warm part of the afternoon had gone away without consulting me. The room had one window with a strip of light beneath the board nailed across it. Dust crossed that strip slowly. Nothing else did.

The coins sat in my palm. Three ordinary-looking ones. One with the damaged king, or damaged person who had perhaps once meant to be a king. Fjord had said they should buy a meal. He had sounded as though he expected the question to end there.

It had not ended there.

The coins were still coins. They had not explained themselves while I was gone.

I put them back in my pocket and stood. My cloak caught on the edge of the chair as I turned. The chair moved with me for one humiliating half-step before the fabric came free.

There was nobody present to see it.

That helped more than it should have.

I checked the cloth tie at my left hip. The bokken sat where it had sat all day, visible enough beneath the cloak that anyone looking would know I had brought a long piece of wood with me. The backpack hung from one shoulder. It was empty in the ordinary way and did not look useful enough to invite questions.

Outside, the town had changed its mind about the day.

The air was cooler. Lamps had begun appearing in windows and over doors. Somewhere beyond the next street, music struck up with more confidence than skill: drums first, then a thin pipe trying to lead them, then voices that knew only the part where a chorus became louder.

People on the road turned the same way.

I waited until three separate groups had done it. A woman with two children. A pair of men carrying cups that sloshed foam onto their hands. A merchant closing the front of his shop while looking over his shoulder at the music.

The carnival was east of Lochward, south bank, after dark. I had names. Now I had a direction.

I joined the outside edge of the moving crowd.

No one took notice of me at first. That was useful. A red cloak did not remain unremarkable forever, but a procession gave every person something brighter to look at. Two painted wagons came through the street ahead, their lanterns hung from poles in uneven rows. A woman in an enormous hat walked backward in front of them, tossing ribbons at children who were too excited to catch them. Somebody had put bells on a mule. The mule was less pleased about this than anyone else.

The route bent around buildings I had not learned names for. I kept the music in front of me and the people between myself and the center of the road. It was enough.

At the bridge, the river came into view below the last slope of street, black except where lantern light broke on it. Beyond it, a tent rose above the far bank in dark blue folds, large enough that the sky seemed to have caught on its ropes.

The crowd thickened near the entrance.

I saw Jester before she saw me. This was mostly because she was pointing at something over Fjord's shoulder with both hands while talking to Beau at a speed that made the rest of them look still. Fjord had one hand near the weapon at his side, not gripping it, only checking where it was as the line pressed around them. Caleb stood with Nott close to his left, both of them angled away from the nearest stranger. Nott's mask made it difficult to tell where she was looking until she moved.

Molly was farther up the entrance lane, bright enough to be part of the lantern display. He held a deck of cards in one hand and spoke to a woman whose expression had the cautious attention of someone deciding whether she had paid to be flattered or insulted. Yasha stood near the opening in the tent wall. A growing stack of things lay beside her: a hunting knife in a leather sheath, a short sword, something that might have been a decorative axe if its owner had wanted to make a point.

The line advanced.

Jester turned, perhaps because Beau said something that made her look for an answer elsewhere. Her face changed as soon as she found me.

“Mielle!” She lifted her arm over the people between us. “You came!”

A few heads turned. Not many.

“I had the address,” I said when I reached speaking distance.

Jester blinked. “You did?”

“East of Lochward. South bank. After dark.”

“Oh.” Her smile widened. “That is technically an address. It is a very bad one, though.”

“It found me anyway.”

Beau looked me over once, beginning with the cloak and ending at the bokken. “You made it through town without buying a map, then.”

“I found a large group of people moving toward the same loud tent.”

“Dangerous method.”

“It has been mostly children so far.”

Fjord gave me a short nod. “Good timing. Line's moving.”

Nott leaned around Caleb's coat. “Did you bring food?”

“No.”

She looked disappointed for the exact amount of time required to make it rude.

“Could have brought bread,” she said.

“I had not considered provisions for the crowd.”

“You should. Crowds eat everything.”

Caleb put a hand lightly against the back of her chairless shoulder as the line shifted. It guided her half a step away from a man with an open purse. She let herself be moved without looking at him.

Jester had already turned toward the front again. “I told you I would get you in. I did not forget.”

“I did not think you had.”

“Good.” She seemed pleased by that. “I would have been very offended by forgetting, but I did not, so now I can be pleased instead.”

The entrance opened in the blue canvas ahead. Bo stood at one side of it, broad as the post beside him, taking coins and looking at each person only long enough to decide whether they were about to create work. Yasha stood at the other.

A man in front of us unsheathed a narrow sword with an expression that suggested he expected everyone to admire it. Yasha held out her hand.

He stopped.

“After the show,” she said.

The man looked at the sword. Then at Yasha. His expression revised itself. He handed it over.

Yasha put it with the others.

That was clear enough.

Fjord moved first when our turn came. He spoke briefly to Bo and paid for himself and the people nearest him. Beau said something under her breath that made Bo's mouth tighten without becoming a smile. Caleb counted coins twice before he handed them over. Nott made a small motion toward the pouch at his belt, then did not touch it.

Jester stepped forward beside me.

“One more,” she said, and dropped the coins into Bo's palm before I could make the moment larger than it was.

Bo looked at me. “Inside. No weapons.”

Jester turned with an expectant little bounce, as though she had completed a trick and wanted to see whether it worked.

“Thank you,” I said.

“Yeah.” She smiled. “Now you get to see the weird animals.”

“I am hopeful they will be normal enough to be disappointing.”

“They probably won't be.”

Yasha had reached for the tie at my hip before I did, then stopped with her hand open between us.

“The sword,” she said.

“Bokken,” I said automatically.

Her eyes went to mine, then to the piece of wood.

“The wooden sword,” she said.

“Yes.”

I turned sideways to loosen the cloth tie. The person behind me moved forward at the same time. The edge of my cloak caught under the strap of their basket. I felt the pull only when I tried to step back.

The basket's owner made a startled sound. Apples rolled against one another inside it.

I stopped. That was preferable to taking the basket with me.

“Sorry,” I said.

“It is all right,” the woman said, though she had to lift the strap free from the fold of my cloak.

Jester put one hand over her mouth. Her eyes had gone bright.

“My cloak has become ambitious,” I said.

That made Jester laugh into her fingers anyway.

Yasha waited without looking impatient. When the cloth came loose, I untied the bokken, held it out with both hands, and let her take it.

Her grip was careful and ordinary. The wood passed from my hands into hers. She set it beside the other surrendered weapons, close enough that I could still see the pale length of it among darker handles and steel.

“After,” she said.

“Thank you.”

Yasha nodded once. Then she bent to take Beau's weapon while Beau objected to something Bo had said about the rule.

The absence at my left side was noticeable for three steps. Not painful. Just wrong in the way a missing button is wrong when your hand reaches for it.

Inside, the tent smelled of damp grass, lamp oil, wool coats and animals kept somewhere beyond the canvas. The floor had not been made into a floor so much as asked to endure a large number of boots. Grass gave way to dirt, then to a patch of loose stone near a central wooden support that held the roof above us.

Lanterns hung from the poles in rings of yellow light. The entrance flap settled behind the last people in line. Across the tent, another flap marked the space where performers came and went. It moved once as someone passed behind it, then went still.

Yasha led Fjord, Jester, Beau, Caleb and Nott toward an open section near the front. They followed in the loose arrangement they had carried from the street: not close enough to look organized, close enough that nobody had to ask where anyone went.

Jester looked back at me from beside Fjord. “There is room here.”

There was room near them. That was not the same thing.

The center of the semicircle had already begun to close around bodies, coats and knees. At the left edge of it, one place remained where the sightline ran past a shoulder instead of directly into someone's back. I pointed at it.

“Here is sufficient.”

Jester considered this. “Okay. But you can still hear me from there.”

“That seems like a mixed blessing.”

She gasped in pleased offense. Beau made a noise that might have been a laugh. Fjord sat down before either of them could make more work out of it.

I took the edge place.

The ground was uneven enough that one knee sat higher than the other. A child two people away was trying to fold a paper animal and failing with complete focus. Behind the performers' flap, something gave a deep, irritated cough. The child looked up. So did half the tent.

Then the lights near the front dimmed one by one.

The last lantern above the entrance remained bright behind us.
