# Planning obligation levels

## Purpose

This file prevents a planning preference, diagnostic, illustrative example or one possible staging choice from becoming an immutable canon rule merely because it was copied through several work artifacts.

Use it when planning, loading out, drafting, auditing or revising a chapter.

## Governing principle

Repetition across work files does not raise authority. A choice written in a plan, mini delta, scene card, loadout and audit is still only as strong as the source that authorized it.

Before prose, classify every result-bearing instruction by obligation level.

## Obligation levels

### Level 1 — Hard authority

A fact or ownership decision fixed by:

- the newest explicit user decision;
- approved chapter text;
- current continuity;
- stable canon or mechanics;
- verified source outcome or authorized Canon Delta.

Examples:

- Jester owns an offer the user explicitly assigned to her;
- no payment occurs;
- an approved legal restriction remains active;
- a power cannot perform an effect outside its documented mechanics.

A Level 1 conflict blocks prose or requires return to the owning authority.

### Level 2 — Required semantic outcome

The meaning or result must reach the page, but exact wording, order, sentence count and minor staging remain free.

Examples:

- the group learns Mielle was using an abandoned building;
- the temporary nature of an offer is clear before acceptance;
- Mielle retains control over disclosure;
- a witness remembers her prior combat without needing a prescribed line.

Audit the delivered meaning, not a copied phrase. A paraphrase passes when the authorized knowledge and consequence remain equivalent.

### Level 3 — Protective constraint

This prevents an unauthorized implication or shortcut. It often passes through omission.

Examples:

- no friendship, romance, debt or party status is promoted;
- no exact shelter location is revealed;
- no capability name or unsupported local knowledge appears;
- no canon character loses source-owned agency.

A Level 3 breach blocks. The prose is not required to announce the constraint.

### Level 4 — Editorial target

A preferred craft direction that may improve rhythm, voice, pressure or clarity but is not a story fact.

Examples:

- uneven cast participation;
- one strong Nott beat rather than several jokes;
- end on a concrete remainder;
- avoid a question-and-answer chain;
- keep one clumsy event rather than repeating the gag.

Divergence is not automatically a blocker. Audit the actual local effect. Raise an issue only when the draft produces a demonstrated failure such as coverage compulsion, flattened dialogue, lost pressure, repetition or tonal damage.

### Level 5 — Free execution

Exact wording, metaphor, sentence rhythm, minor movement, optional reaction, object vocabulary, silence and non-outcome staging that do not change authority, knowledge, mechanics, relationship status or material consequence.

A work artifact may suggest an option, but it must not later audit that suggestion as mandatory merely because it was written down.

## Audit severity rule

Use severity from actual damage, not from distance from the plan.

- **Blocker:** violates Level 1, Level 2 or Level 3 in a way that changes authority, knowledge, mechanics, relationship state, legal state, causal result or approval safety.
- **Major:** produces a sustained structural or voice failure that materially weakens the scene but does not change hard story facts.
- **Important:** local clarity, rhythm, evidence or character-specificity problem worth revising.
- **Optional:** preference, alternative staging or line-level taste with no demonstrated damage.

A Level 4 target can become major only when local evidence shows a real cluster. It cannot be called a blocker merely because the draft chose another valid execution.

## Character participation rule

Presence does not create an obligation to speak. A decision-relevant character must retain owned action or consequence. An optional line may remain for rhythm, texture or relationship color even when it is removable, provided that:

- it is character-specific;
- it does not steal ownership;
- it does not introduce unsupported knowledge;
- it does not accumulate into cast coverage;
- the plan does not falsely label it required.

Removal and replacement tests are diagnostics. They become blockers only when a supposedly required or result-bearing beat fails them, or when repeated optional beats create a demonstrated structural failure.

## Wording and vocabulary rule

A preferred word is not a lexical prohibition unless stable canon or an explicit user decision says it is.

Audit whether wording changes identity, knowledge or meaning. Do not block a natural synonym that preserves the authorized result.

## Version-comparison rule

When comparing drafts, distinguish:

- lost hard fact or semantic result;
- changed protective boundary;
- changed but equivalent wording;
- editorial gain/loss;
- neutral execution change.

Do not label every difference specificity regression.

## Planning receipt

Every chapter plan and mini delta should include an obligation map with:

| Instruction | Authority source | Level | Required meaning | Free execution | Audit consequence |

|  |  | 1–5 |  |  | blocker / evidence-based craft review / none |

## Stop condition

Planning is ready when:

- Level 1–3 controls are clear and non-conflicting;
- Level 4 targets are not disguised as immutable results;
- Level 5 choices remain genuinely free;
- character participation is pressure-selected rather than form-selected;
- the audit can distinguish factual failure from editorial preference.
